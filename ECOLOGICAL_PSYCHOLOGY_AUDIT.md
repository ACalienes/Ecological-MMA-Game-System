## 1. Core Principles Digest

### 1.1 Animal–environment mutuality & ecological scale
The foundational assumption: **the animal–environment system is the fundamental unit of analysis** (Ecological Assumption 5, p. 57). You cannot understand the animal without its environment, nor the environment without the animal that inhabits it. The environment is not a "factor" added to an equation — it is *part of the system from the start* (p. 57). Fred's fear on a rickety bridge is nested in the *Fred-bridge system* because he *cannot fly*; his particular action capabilities constrain the meaning of the event (p. 57).

A second move at this level: **choose the appropriate variables and the appropriate scale.** Ecological psychology rejects reductionism — the whole is different from the sum of its parts (Gestalt point, p. 58, pointillist-painting figure 4.3). It studies *macroscopic, ecological* variables, not the lower-order variables of Newtonian physics / Euclidean geometry (p. 58). "If the answer is still ambiguous, you are probably still standing too close" (p. 58). Crucially, ecological theory is built from *everyday successes*, not artificial failures/illusions (p. 59) — an evolved organism is presumed *good* at the tasks that keep it alive.

A third move: **embrace nonlocal, nonlinear causality** (p. 59–61). Linear causality (hold all else constant, manipulate one thing) "may seem like the rule, but it is the exception" (Turvey, 2013; p. 59). Behavior is multicausal; causality is often "deeply nonobvious" (Gottlieb; Box 4.1, p. 60). And the body should be treated as a **system, not a machine** (p. 61–64): a machine's parts function independently of context and are interchangeable; a system is "a collection of small, simple elements performing small, simple tasks in response to local constraints and stimuli — and from this collection… complex behavior will emerge" (p. 61). The metronomes-on-a-board example (p. 61–62, fig. 4.4): to change the metronomes' synchronization, you don't change the metronomes — you change the *constraint* (flip the cans so the board can't move). **You change behavior by changing constraints, not components.**

### 1.2 Direct perception & the rejection of the central executive
Traditional theories posit a **central executive** that receives ambiguous stimulation, "fixes" it, and infers the world (Ch. 1–2). Blau & Wagman banish it: it requires a *loan of intelligence* (it must already be smart to do its job), suffers the *outness problem* and the *feedback problem*, and — fatally — **lacks explanatory power** (p. 56, fig. 4.1). Positing it just "pushes the question back a step" (what moved Claudia's central executive?). Perception is instead **direct**: the animal detects lawfully structured information; nothing needs to be processed, only detected (p. 82; Box 5.1, p. 87–88). *Direct does not mean unmediated by tools* — perception through an interface or a hand-held rod is still direct, because directness is about whether perception is mediated by something *mental* (a representation/inference), not something physical (p. 219, ecological interface design).

### 1.3 Information, invariants, specification
Animals are immersed in an **ocean of energy** — patterns of light, sound, and physical forces (p. 82). This energy is not uniform; surface and substance properties *lawfully structure* it (p. 82, p. 91–93). At any **point of observation** an animal encounters a *unique* distribution of structured energy (the **ambient optic array** for light; p. 93). Because the structuring is *lawful (unambiguous)*, the energy pattern **specifies** — carries *information about* — the affordances of surrounding surfaces (p. 82). The relevant variables are **higher-order invariants**, not lower-order properties (p. 82; p. 218, ecological interface design displays invariants like optic flow / time-to-contact, not raw data).

Key information variables named in the book:
- **Tau (τ):** the relative rate of optical expansion of an object's texture; specifies **time-to-arrival/time-to-contact**, assuming current conditions continue (p. 74, p. 165–166, p. 219–220). Used for braking, catching, defensive blinking, landing a drone, and "distance to break" in surgery.
- **Global optic flow:** specifies self-motion and how to control it; rate of flow = movement speed (Ch. 6; p. 147–148 moving room; p. 211 Chicago Lake Shore Drive lines that increase flow rate to make drivers slow down).
- **Inertia tensor (I):** for **dynamic/effortful touch** — the resistance to rotation in different directions at a grasp point specifies the length, orientation, and "hit-with-able-ness" of a wielded object (p. 123–127, fig. 8.2). Perceived length tracks the major principal moment of inertia (I_xx), *not* geometric length directly (p. 127, fig. 8.4).

Lawfulness implies the **same information is available across energy forms** (multiple perceptual modalities can specify the *same* affordance — p. 83) and **across nervous systems** (the details of the brain are "potentially irrelevant"; ecological psychology is a psychology for *all animals* — p. 84).

### 1.4 Affordances — definition, measurement, body-/action-scaling
**Affordances are opportunities for behavior that emerge from the relationship between animal and environment** (Gibson, 1979; Ch. 5, p. 73–89). They are **neither purely objective (a property of the environment) nor purely subjective (in the animal)** — like friction, they emerge from a *relationship between two surfaces* (p. 75, fig. 5.3). "Hide-behind-able" exists for the cat but not the dog, and only when there is both an organism and a place to hide (p. 75). Affordances are **activity-specific *meanings* of the environment** (Turvey; p. 78) — and because meaning is in the relationship, *meanings can be perceived* (p. 78).

Properties of affordances:
- **Relational & animal-relative.** What is leap-on-to-able for a cat is not for a dog, nor for an *ailing* cat vs. a healthy one, nor a *stout* cat vs. a svelte one (p. 76). Affordances differ between species, between individuals, and **for the same animal in different contexts or at different moments** (a puddle affords jumping when running but not when standing still — p. 76, fig. 5.4).
- **Perceiving affordances = perceiving the (im)possible future.** You "perceive the future all the time" — catching a ball requires perceiving where it *will be*; performing any behavior requires perceiving whether/when/how it *could* be performed *before* attempting it (p. 74).
- **Multiple simultaneous fit.** An affordance is usually *not* a fit between one property of the animal and one of the environment; it requires *several* criteria met *simultaneously* (a surface affords standing only if sufficiently horizontal AND flat AND rigid AND extended — jigsaw figure 5.5, p. 77). Miss any one and the affordance is absent.
- **Body-scaled (measured by critical π ratios).** Warren's (1984) stair-climbing studies are the paradigm (p. 78–81). Whether a step is "step-on-able" by stepping depends not on absolute step height but on the **ratio of step height to leg length**. Plotted against absolute height, tall and short people give different curves; plotted against the *ratio*, the curves overlap — a single critical π value of **0.88** (above which stepping becomes impossible), and the *perceived* boundary matched the biomechanical one (p. 79–80, fig. 5.8). Same logic for crossing gaps (leg length; p. 84), fitting through apertures (shoulder width; critical ratio ≈1.16–1.3; p. 86), and reaching (arm length; p. 88).
- **Action-scaled (scaled to the ability to do work).** Warren's 2nd/3rd experiments (p. 81): the *optimal* step (least effortful, measured by O₂ consumption) occurred at a ratio of **0.26** of leg length, and people could *perceive* which stairs were most efficient — perception is scaled not just to whether you *can* act but to *how effortful/efficient* it would be.
- **Preferred boundary ≠ maximum boundary.** People transition from one-hand to two-hand grasp, or from arm-reach to leaning, *before* their absolute limit — at a comfortable "preferred reaching boundary" (Mark et al.; p. 88). Rats and hamsters press levers at heights *below* their maximum (p. 89).
- **Dynamic & animal-plus-object scaling.** Affordances depend on how the body *moves* (a moving body takes up more space than a still one — passing-through is scaled to standing-height + vertical bob; p. 86), and on objects attached to the body — affordances are for the **animal-plus-object** system (carrying groceries, wearing armor/football pads, using a wheelchair, hermit-crab shells; p. 86–87, fig. 5.11).
- **Perceiving an affordance does not require perceiving its component properties.** An affordance is perceived "in and of itself" — improving perception of leg length doesn't transfer to improving perception of jump-and-reach affordance (Box 5.1, p. 87–88). *You perceive the opportunity, not the measurements.*

### 1.5 Perception–action coupling & the perceptual loop
Perception's purpose is to **guide doing** — "getting light into the *muscles*" (Turvey's reframing; Ch. 6 p. 90; Ch. 9 p. 139). Perceiving and acting are **continuous, not separate sequential stages** — a Möbius-strip relationship (p. 192, fig. 12.1). Perceiving is an **activity that unfolds over time**: information (e.g., τ, or I in the inertial array) "can be detected only as the animal or an object moves" (p. 207). Action generates the information that guides further action. This loop *cannot be decomposed* without destroying the information.

### 1.6 Action, coordination, Bernstein's degrees-of-freedom problem, synergies
Controlling the body via individual anatomical units is a **nonstarter**: one arm alone involves 7 joint degrees of freedom and ~26 muscles → ~4×10²⁶ combinations; the whole body has 600+ muscles (p. 142–143, figs. 9.2–9.3). This is the **Bernstein degrees-of-freedom problem** (p. 143).

**The solution has two parts:**
1. **Forces for free.** Movements occur *in* a world and *with* a body that both obey physics, so many forces are provided gratis — gravity, reactive forces, the body's connectivity. "Walking is merely controlled falling" (p. 143). Coordinated movement is about *capitalizing on and cooperating with* free forces — "more like sculpting… than like building" (p. 144).
2. **Synergies (coordinative structures).** A **synergy** is "a grouping of potentially independent anatomical components that work together as a functional unit in exploiting information about a given affordance" — a "choir of muscles that harmonize" (Bernstein/Turvey; p. 144). Synergies are **soft-assembled** (temporary, task-specific, dissolve after use), **goal-preserving and self-correcting** (the jaw-tug speech experiment: when the jaw is perturbed mid-syllable, the *lips* compensate for "b" but the *tongue* compensates for "z" — the synergy preserves the goal, not a fixed set of muscles; Kelso; p. 146), and produce **micro-level variability with macro-level consistency** (Bernstein's blacksmiths: joint trajectories vary strike to strike, but the *hammer-head trajectory* stays consistent; p. 145, fig. 9.4).

**Repetition without repetition** (Bernstein; p. 145): "No two instances of a given behavior are exactly the same. They can't be. The context will always differ." Therefore *learning* a skill may require practicing the *same* movement in the *same* context, but **mastering** a skill requires practicing achieving the goal using *different* sets of behaviors across *different* contexts (p. 145; reinforced by Ch. 10 Box 10.2 — robots trained on highly *variable* infant walking paths beat robots trained on regular paths 2,888–75 in a 4,000-game tournament; p. 169–170).

**Postural & suprapostural synergies** (p. 146–152): Standing is "the prevention of falling"; it is a *balancing* task built from synergies fed by optic flow (the moving-room paradigm: subtle room motion makes people unconsciously sway in sync; p. 147–148). Postures are organized to support *suprapostural* goals — sway *decreases* in directions that would disrupt a task and *increases* where it wouldn't, even when subjects are told to "stand still" (laser-pointing study; p. 150). **Posture and the behaviors it supports are cooperative, not competitive** (p. 149, 151).

**Coordination dynamics — in-phase/anti-phase.** Two basic, most-stable coordination patterns: **in-phase** (limbs move same direction, same time) and **anti-phase** (opposite). In-phase is *more stable* than anti-phase; at high speed, anti-phase spontaneously *switches* to in-phase but not vice versa (the finger-tapping / Fred-and-Claudia hand-puppets demonstration; p. 153–155, fig. 9.8). Gaits are coordination patterns with **nonlinear phase transitions** between them (walk→run; horse walk→trot→gallop; p. 152–153). This is the **HKB model** (Haken-Kelso-Bunz, 1985; p. 155).

### 1.7 Constraints
The book doesn't use Newell's three-category taxonomy by name, but it is everywhere in substance: behavior is channeled by constraints, and **you change behavior by changing constraints** (metronomes, p. 61; soup-can orientation as a constraint, p. 62). Constraints set up *which* attractor layout exists (Ch. 14). The most important corrective the book offers our system: **a constraint is not a rule that prescribes — it reduces the configuration space so that functional behavior *emerges***. Compare "designate exact outlet locations along a wall" (other-organization, one acceptable outcome) vs. "there should be power outlets every 12 feet" (a local rule — "an infinite number of possible outcomes, and the solution will emerge" — p. 271). *Good constraints are the second kind.*

### 1.8 Dynamical systems — attractors, order/control parameters, layouts
Behavior is modeled as a **ball on an attractor layout** (the sheet game, p. 234–237, fig. 14.1, 14.3). Locations on the sheet = possible behaviors; the ball = current behavior; basins = **attractors** (stable behaviors that "attract nearby behaviors the way a magnet attracts iron"; the deepest attractor is the most stable and usually the most *efficient*; Box 14.1, p. 237).
- **Types of attractors** (Box 14.1, p. 237–238): **fixed-point** (settles to a state), **periodic** (repeats a cycle, e.g., a pendulum clock), **chaotic** (rule-governed, deterministic, fractal, sensitive to initial conditions — *not random*), and **repellers** (states the system avoids).
- **Order parameter (collective variable):** the macro variable that captures the coordination state — e.g., **relative phase** between two oscillators (the HKB φ; p. 251 note 2). This is what you measure to see the behavior.
- **Control parameter:** a variable that, when changed, reorganizes the layout — e.g., **movement speed** in HKB makes the anti-phase attractor vanish (p. 250); ΔT in convection cells; drainpipe size in the bathtub vortex (p. 274). *Changing a control parameter changes the layout; it does not directly dictate the new behavior.*
- **Layouts change three ways** (p. 238–242): **death of an attractor** (a stable pattern destabilizes/disappears — forgetting, breaking a habit, the anti-phase attractor at speed), **birth of an attractor** (learning a genuinely new pattern — juggling study, Beek & Santvoord; p. 240), and **movement of an attractor** (**adaptation** — adjusting an existing skill to a new context, e.g., prism-glasses throwing; p. 241). **Learning = new attractor; adaptation = moved attractor** — an important distinction (p. 241).
- **Noise is functional, not error** (p. 242–243). Noise constantly perturbs the ball; it is what lets a system escape a shallow/local attractor and find a deeper/global one (shaking the sheet helps the ball find the deepest basin; added noise made participants *more* likely to discover the efficient gear-systems strategy; p. 243). Pink noise is the signature of a healthy, poised, flexible system (p. 200–201).
- **Other dynamical phenomena:** **hysteresis** (the system's current state depends on its history/trajectory — a "memory"; p. 250), **equifinality** (different starting points → same end state, because of one strong attractor; p. 243–244), **pluripotentiality** (same start → different ends, when balanced between attractors + noise; p. 244–245).

### 1.9 Self-organization & emergence
**Self-organization** is the alternative to *other*-organization (a central executive, a blueprint, a conductor) — and it is the rule, not the exception, in biological systems (Ch. 16, p. 270–285). Definition (p. 270–272):
1. **Not other-organized** — no external leader, no blueprint, no rigid hierarchy.
2. **Components are minimally intelligent** — they follow simple rules; no loan of intelligence (p. 271).
3. **Rules are local** — followed using only local information, requiring **constant mutual interaction** among components (remove the interaction and the pattern stops — metronomes on a non-transmitting surface don't synchronize; p. 271–272).
4. **Patterns emerge** — a nonrandom configuration comprehensible only at a *larger scale* than the parts, and *not predictable* from the parts (p. 272).

Characteristics (p. 272–284): self-organized systems are **dynamic** (always changing), **recover from perturbations** (the orchestra player who loses their place re-synchronizes with neighbors; p. 272–273), are **multistable** (more than one stable emergent state — convection rolls RLR vs LRL, bathtub vortex CW vs CCW; p. 273–274), are **multirealizable** (the same parts reorganize for many goals — your legs can walk, kick, skate, arabesque; the slime mold is single-cell, worm, plant, spore at different life stages; p. 274, p. 280–281), and show **parameter tuning** (control parameters; p. 274). Examples: ant foraging converging on the shortest path to the best food via pheromone local rules (p. 275–276), firefly synchronization via two control parameters (variability of flashing + coupling strength; Box 16.1, p. 276–278), heartbeat (a **partially** self-organized system — local "beat + sync with neighbors" rules plus an SAN pacemaker; fibrillation is the *other* stable attractor; p. 282–284), and self-organizing robots that **build whole houses from simple local rules** (Wyss Institute; Box 16.2, p. 285). The deep lesson (p. 284): a wise designer gives contractors *a list of rules and lets them adapt to local circumstances* rather than a single rigid blueprint — "the houses will be far more adapted to their circumstances… and you (the divine architect) will have to do a lot less work."

### 1.10 Learning — education of attention, education of intention, calibration, perceptual differentiation
Learning is **the fine-tuning of perceiving-and-acting abilities**, not the accumulation of facts or the enrichment of representations (Gibson & Pick; p. 204). Four named processes (Jacobs & Michaels, 2007; p. 204–207):
- **Education of intention** — learning *which behaviors to attempt* (and therefore which affordances to perceive) and how to explore to uncover information about them (p. 204).
- **Education of attention** — learning *which* components of a structured energy array provide the *most useful* information about an intended affordance, and shifting toward them and away from less-useful ones (over repeated swat attempts the cat attends to the dog's τ rather than its distance/speed; p. 204–205). Demonstrated in the wielding study (Arzamarski et al.): subtle shifts toward the informative component of I when perceiving a learned property, dramatic shifts when intending a new property (p. 205–206).
- **Calibration** — learning *how to use* the detected information to scale perception/action correctly (the mapping between information and action; p. 206). **Calibration is specific and does NOT transfer across action modes** — subjects calibrated to a visual drift while *walking* did **not** transfer that calibration to *throwing or kicking*, because what they learned was "a specific relationship between optic flow direction and walking," not a general fact about the world (Bruggeman & Warren; p. 206–207).
- **Perceptual differentiation** (development; Ch. 10) — sensitivity to an informative variable *develops* over time; e.g., children become increasingly sensitive to rotational inertia (I) into early adulthood (p. 168–169).

**The single most important learning finding for us** (Ch. 10, p. 159–161): **animals learn *specific* affordances, not general facts.** Infants who learned which slopes afford safe *crawling* did **not** transfer that to *walking* — they "started over from zero" after a new motor skill, because the *information* about affordances for crawling is *entirely different* from that for walking (different limbs, different optic flow, different base of support; p. 160–161). And experience does not make babies "afraid of heights" — it makes them better at perceiving *affordances* in *context* (Box 10.1, p. 161). Action capabilities and the affordances available change *each other* continuously (p. 158, fig. 10.2; "developmental cascade"). Older adults' perceptual boundaries shift *with* their reduced action capabilities (stair π ratio 0.90 young vs 0.70 old; p. 162).

### 1.11 Perceiving & acting WITH others — joint action, social affordances, social synergies
**The same lawful principles that govern animal–environment systems govern animal–animal–environment systems** (Ch. 11, p. 172–191). The book frames combat directly. Three pillars:
- **Perceiving affordances *for others*.** The same properties of an animal that structure the energy *it* encounters also structure the energy *other* animals encounter — so a pattern carries information about affordances *for the self* AND *for others* (p. 175). People accurately perceive another person's max standing-reach and max jump-reach (within 8%; p. 179). This is **learned from watching the other move**: watching someone *walk* informs their jump-and-reach ability (because both require vertical leg forces), but watching them *twist* does not (p. 180–181). Sport-specific: **basketball players are better than non-players at perceiving jump-and-reach affordances for others** — but no better at sitting/standing-reach affordances (Weast et al.; p. 181–182). *Domain-specific experience tunes perception of opponents' specific affordances.*
- **Interpersonal affordances are action-scaled and *emergent*, not additive.** Whether two people grasp a plank together vs. solo transitions at a critical π ratio (≈0.98) just like one person's one-hand→two-hand transition (p. 182–183). When two people walk through a doorway together they turn shoulders at *less* than the sum of their individual widths (1.13× combined vs 1.22× individual) — they **share personal space**; affordances for the pair are an **emergent property**, not the sum of individual affordances (p. 184–185).
- **Social (interpersonal) synergies.** Two people coordinating create a synergy that *spans the individuals* — the same in-phase/anti-phase dynamics and HKB stability apply (rocking chairs synchronize **unintentionally** when people can see each other but not when they look away; people walking together fall into a four-legged "pace" or "trot" gait; p. 186–191). **Coordination is driven by *detecting lawfully structured information about each other*, not by mirror neurons / shared representations** (Box 11.1, p. 177–178).
- **The combat-sport gem (Box 11.2, p. 188–189):** In kendo, competitors exhibit **two preferred interpersonal distances** — a **near** distance that affords offense and a **far** distance that affords defense — and they **avoid the middle distance that affords neither**. More-experienced competitors spend *more* time at the near (offensive) distance because they can move into striking position faster. A competitor's decision to step toward/away from an opponent is based on **perceived affordances for striking that opponent.** The rugby VR study shows the same: players choose to run vs. pass based on affordances for advancing the ball through gaps — for themselves *and* teammates — and experienced players do it better (p. 189).

### 1.12 Applications (Ch. 13) — gems for environment/interface design
- **Design affordances *into* the environment.** If affordances can be perceived, they can be *designed*: a door handle that clearly affords pulling-not-pushing, furniture that affords supported standing instead of sitting ("The End of Sitting," Rietveld; p. 212–216). Conversely, *hidden/ambiguous* affordances are dangerous (the "bottom of flight" illusion — a same-texture bottom stair causing a fall; p. 213–214).
- **Manipulate information to compel behavior without instruction.** Chicago painted lines that increase the rate of global optic flow as drivers approach a curve, *compelling them to slow down* with no conscious thought — 36% crash reduction (p. 211, fig. 13.2). **Information design beats instruction.**
- **Ecological Interface Design (EID)** (Vicente & Rasmussen; p. 217–219): display higher-order *invariants/relationships* (optic flow, τ) as an interactive dynamic display, not raw data dials — "more like playing a video game than doing a complex math problem." Training focuses on education of intention/attention, not memorization.
- **Tools become part of the perceiving-acting system.** A skilled user fuses with the tool — the controller, white cane, rod, or racket "feels like a part of him"; the unit is the person-tool-environment system (p. 196–197 video-game controller; p. 219 drone; surgical tools p. 213, 219–220). Directly relevant to grappling grips, gloves, and the cage.

## 2. Implementation Mapping — Principle → Our System

For each principle: how it should concretely shape game design, what athletes should attune to, win conditions, vocabulary, coaching cues, and learning-path logic, tied to our files.

### 2.1 Animal–environment mutuality → the fighter-opponent-cage system is the unit
- **Design:** Every game must include the opponent and the relevant environmental constraints (cage edge, range, surface). Our games already do this well: `tight-block.md` and `the-square.md` both make the **square boundary a live constraint** ("both feet out = instant loss"), which is the book's mutuality principle made physical (the boundary is *part of the fighter-system*, like Fred's bridge — p. 57). Keep this.
- **Vocabulary:** Adopt "the **stylistic matchup** is the unit of analysis, not the fighter" — `LANGUAGE.md` already says this for analysis ("The actual subject of analysis, not the names"). Extend the same discipline to *training*: never write a game spec around one fighter in isolation.
- **Scale discipline:** When writing a game, describe behavior at the *macroscopic* scale (reads, distance, pressure, off-balancing) — never at the joint-angle scale. "If the answer is still ambiguous, you are probably still standing too close" (p. 58).

### 2.2 Direct perception / no central executive → train detection, not computation
- **Coaching cues:** Cues should **direct attention to information**, never prescribe a computation or an internal body-part movement. Our `implementation.md` external-focus table is correct and aligns with this. Tight Block's cue "**See center mass** — the eyes lie… keeps shoulder & hip motion in view" is exactly an education-of-attention cue toward the informative variable. This is excellent; make it the template.
- **Correction:** Drop any language implying the fighter "reads then decides then reacts" as sequential stages — perception and action are one loop (§2.5).

### 2.3 Information / invariants / specification → name what athletes attune to
- **Design:** For every game, explicitly list the **information variables** the athlete should attune to, in the book's terms where they map:
  - **τ (time-to-contact):** closing distance on a strike, a shot, a level change — the defender should attune to the *rate of expansion* of the opponent (and the opponent's limbs), not their absolute distance/speed. This is the deep justification for Tight Block's "read rhythm/timing" and The Square's "see the shoulder and hip motion."
  - **Global optic flow:** the fighter's own movement/closing pressure; cage-edge approach.
  - **Inertia tensor / dynamic touch (§2.8 below):** grips, frames, clinch — what the hands *feel*.
- Our `key-concepts.md` "Information Sources in Combat Sports" table (visual/haptic/proprioceptive/acoustic) is good but **generic**. Upgrade it to name **τ and global optic flow** as the *specific* visual invariants, and the **inertial array** as the haptic invariant — that is what the book actually says specifies affordances (p. 82, 123–127, 165–166).

### 2.4 Affordances → win conditions, scoring, and level design built on critical ratios
This is the richest mapping.
- **Affordances are relational & contextual** → our affordance vocabulary (`key-concepts.md`: strikeability, takedown-ability, submission-ability, escape-ability, counter-ability) is sound and matches Gibson. **Keep the "-able" naming** — the book confirms it ("adding the suffix '-able' is often all that is required to shift from talking about a behavior to talking about an affordance," p. 73). Tight Block's `emma-flow` "Constraints → Affordances" block is a faithful instantiation.
- **Body-scaling (critical π) → individualize constraints by ratio, not absolute.** This is the biggest *new* actionable idea the book gives us. When a game sets a distance, a target height, an aperture, or a "no continuous backing up" zone, scale it to the athlete's **reach/leg-length/shoulder-width ratio**, not an absolute number. E.g., the "close range" start in Tight Block should be defined as a *ratio of the defender's arm length* (the range at which blocking is the live affordance), so a 5'5" wrestler and a 6'2" kickboxer (our `pitfalls.md` Pitfall 5 example) get the *same relational problem*. Our docs say "individualize" but never say **how** — the answer is **critical-ratio scaling** (Warren, p. 79–81).
- **Action-scaling & preferred boundaries → reward the efficient/comfortable solution, not just the possible one.** The book shows perception is scaled to *effort/efficiency* and that skilled actors transition at a *preferred* (comfortable) boundary before their max (p. 81, 88). For us: scoring should reward **economical** solutions (the dexterity criterion "rationally/economically," `key-concepts.md`) and games should let athletes find their *preferred* transition point (e.g., when to switch from striking to clinch, from one grip to two) rather than forcing the absolute-limit solution. The Square's phase transitions (survive → counter → off-balance → finish) are a good scaffold for this; make the *trigger* to advance a phase the athlete's own perceived preferred boundary, not a coach command.
- **Multiple simultaneous fit → don't isolate single properties in scoring.** "Strikeability" is a simultaneous fit (open line AND in range AND off-balance opponent AND your base set). Avoid scoring single cues ("hands dropped = strike") that the book warns are not the affordance (Box 5.1, p. 87–88). Score the *taken opportunity*, not the isolated property.
- **Dynamic & animal-plus-object scaling → train with the gear and in motion.** Affordances change with gloves, with fatigue (body-in-motion takes more space, p. 86), and with weapons/implements. Our `the-square.md` already escalates to "shot/clinch threat" and weighting — good. Add: explicitly note that a *moving, swaying* fighter occupies more space than a static one (relevant to cage-cutting and aperture/angle games).

### 2.5 Perception–action coupling & the perceptual loop → never decompose
- **Design rule:** Maintain the loop in every task. Our `environment-design.md` "task simplification vs. decomposition" distinction is **correct and well-grounded** (Bernstein/Gibson). The book's own example — perceiving information "can be detected only as the animal or an object moves" (p. 207) — is the deep justification: a static, unopposed drill removes the very information the skill needs.
- **Coaching:** Frame perception and action as one continuous activity (Möbius strip, p. 192), not "read → react." Edit any doc that implies sequential stages.

### 2.6 Degrees of freedom / synergies / repetition-without-repetition → the core learning engine
- **Design:** Games should let **synergies soft-assemble** under constraints rather than prescribe a fixed movement. "Defend the takedown — points for staying standing" (our `mma-applications.md`) is right because it lets a *sprawl/underhook/lateral* synergy assemble to the goal; "when they shoot you must sprawl" is wrong (prescribes anatomy). Keep this; it is the book's "synergy preserves the *goal*, not a fixed set of muscles" (jaw-tug study, p. 146).
- **Forces for free → cue cooperation with physics.** Tight Block's "**Base under fire** — create suspension like an ATV over bumps" and The Square's "**redirect directional force** rather than absorb it" are textbook *forces-for-free* cues (capitalize on reactive forces, controlled falling; p. 143–144). Excellent — make this a standard cue category.
- **Repetition without repetition → variability is the feature, not the bug.** Our `environment-design.md` Principle 4 is faithful. Strengthen it with the book's strongest evidence: variable infant-walking-trained robots crushed regular-trained robots (p. 169–170). For learning paths: **early/coordination stage** = repeat the *same* problem to assemble the synergy; **mastery/adaptability stage** = repeat the *goal* across *varied* opponents/contexts (p. 145). The Square's "add one variable at a time" and "fill the class's collective deficiency" are good operationalizations.
- **In-phase/anti-phase & gait transitions → expect and exploit nonlinear switches.** Stance, rhythm, and gait are coordination patterns with abrupt transitions under a control parameter (speed/pressure). Coaches should expect a fighter's pattern to *suddenly* reorganize under pressure (not degrade smoothly) and design games that push toward beneficial transitions. (See §2.8 dynamical mapping.)

### 2.7 Constraints → constrain to *afford*, never to *prescribe*
- Our `environment-design.md` Principle 2 ("Constrain to Afford") and `pitfalls.md` Pitfall 1 ("Over-Constraining") are **correct and the most important methodology we have.** The book's outlet analogy (p. 271) is a perfect teaching example to add: a good constraint is "outlets every 12 feet" (emergent, many solutions), not "outlet exactly here" (one solution).
- **Co-adaptation** (`environment-design.md`): constrain the *attacker* to make the *defender* learn. This is sound and matches the book's animal-animal-environment framing (Ch. 11). The Square does this ("offense must allow the game to play — no teeing off").

### 2.8 Dynamical systems → model fighters as attractor layouts; design control parameters
This is **under-developed in our docs** and is a major opportunity.
- **Reframe skill as an attractor layout.** A fighter's habitual patterns are **attractors** (deep = stable = efficient = default under pressure). A game's job is to **reshape the layout**: deepen a desired attractor or destabilize an unwanted one.
- **Distinguish learning from adaptation** (p. 241): **birth of an attractor** = teaching a genuinely new pattern (new game, lots of exploration, expect instability); **movement of an attractor** = adapting an existing skill to a new opponent/context (fight-camp work). Our `session-design.md` periodization table should adopt this language explicitly.
- **Control parameters = the dials we turn.** Speed, pressure, range, fatigue, and number of opponents are **control parameters** that reorganize the layout (HKB speed makes anti-phase vanish, p. 250). Our "variability dial" and "representativeness dial" (`session-design.md`) are control parameters — name them as such. Pushing a control parameter past a critical value produces a **nonlinear** behavior switch (this is why "minor rule changes can revolutionize fighting styles," which `theory.md` asserts but doesn't ground — now grounded at p. 250).
- **Order parameter = what we measure.** For coordination/timing games, the order parameter is relative phase / timing between fighter and opponent. Define *what you measure* per game (the book's RQA/phase-space methods, p. 246, are how researchers infer the layout — we measure proxies: did the read happen, did the synergy hold, did the transition occur).
- **Noise is productive → keep useful chaos.** Our "amber zone / productive instability" (`session-design.md`, `pitfalls.md`) is the book's **functional noise** (p. 242–243) — perturbation that lets the system escape a shallow attractor and find a deeper one. This is well-aligned; cite the gear-systems finding (added noise → more discovery, p. 243) as justification.
- **Hysteresis → respect history.** A fighter's current state depends on their trajectory (p. 250); a pattern entered under pressure persists. Sessions should account for path-dependence (the GREEN→AMBER→GREEN flow in `session-design.md` is implicitly using this — name it).

### 2.9 Self-organization & emergence → the coach is a rule-giver, not a choreographer
- Our `theory.md` and `index.md` correctly list self-organization. The book sharpens the **definition** in ways we should adopt: components follow **local rules**, require **constant interaction**, and patterns **emerge at a larger scale** (p. 270–272). For us, the "components" are the fighter's synergies and the two-fighter dyad; the "local rules" are the game's constraints; the emergent pattern is the fighting style.
- **The house-building lesson (p. 284, Box 16.2):** the coach should give **a list of rules and let athletes adapt to local circumstances**, not a single rigid blueprint — "the houses will be far more adapted… and you will have to do a lot less work." This is the single best one-line articulation of our entire philosophy; quote it in `implementation.md`.
- **Multistability/multirealizability → degeneracy.** Our "degeneracy" concept (`key-concepts.md`) IS the book's multirealizability (same parts → many goals; many solutions → one goal; p. 274). Cross-reference them. Train varied opponents to build multiple stable solutions (our `mma-applications.md` "Build Degeneracy" section is correct).
- **Perturbation recovery → train the re-organize, not just the pattern.** The orchestra player who loses their place and re-syncs (p. 272–273) is the model: games should *perturb* a working pattern and reward **re-establishment** (Tight Block's "recovers structure quickly," The Square's clinch-tolerance work). Score the recovery.

### 2.10 Learning → build learning paths on education of attention/intention + calibration, and respect non-transfer
- **Learning-path logic:** Sequence games to (1) educate **intention** (which behavior/affordance to go for), (2) educate **attention** (which information specifies it — e.g., "see center mass," attune to τ), (3) **calibrate** (scale the response correctly via feedback). Our `implementation.md` feedback section (KR/KP, questioning, reducing feedback over time) supports calibration — tie it explicitly to the calibration concept (p. 206).
- **CRITICAL non-transfer rule:** Calibration and affordance-perception are **specific to the action mode and do not transfer** (crawling→walking, p. 160; walking-calibration→throwing, p. 206). **Implication for our library:** do *not* assume a skill learned in one game/position transfers to a different action mode. A fighter who has learned to read τ for *striking* defense has *not* thereby learned to read τ for *takedown* defense — these require re-learning because the information is different. **Build separate learning paths per action mode and verify transfer empirically** (our `implementation.md` "Transfer Assessment" is the right tool — now grounded in why it's necessary). The Square's explicit phase-by-phase scaffolding (striking → clinch → takedown) is *exactly* the right structure *because* it re-educates attention at each new action mode rather than assuming carry-over.
- **Differentiation develops slowly** (p. 168) — sensitivity to subtle information (e.g., grip pressure / I) matures over long timescales; don't expect beginners to perceive what experts perceive.

### 2.11 Perceiving/acting with others → the opponent-reading engine
- **This is the most MMA-specific chapter and our docs under-use it.** Add a "Perceiving Affordances for the Opponent" section to `mma-applications.md`:
  - A fighter must perceive affordances **for themselves AND for the opponent** simultaneously (p. 175) — "what can I do" *and* "what can they do." Win conditions should reward reading the opponent's *affordances* (e.g., recognizing when the opponent is in a position that affords *them* a shot).
  - **Preferred interpersonal distances (the kendo finding, p. 188–189) is gold for MMA.** Design distance-management games around **two preferred ranges** — a near range that affords offense and a far range that affords defense — and the **dead middle that affords neither**. Reward fighters for *living* in their advantageous range and *avoiding* the neutral zone. This directly grounds `LANGUAGE.md`'s "Distance management… the substrate underneath striking output" in primary theory. Experienced fighters should spend *more* time in their offensive range (they enter faster) — make that a progression marker.
  - **Domain-specific perceptual learning of opponents:** watching an opponent *move* (walk, shift weight) informs their *striking/shooting* affordances (p. 180–181). Game implication: tape study / live reading should focus on movement that *shares force dynamics* with the threat (level change ↔ shot), not irrelevant movement.
  - **Social synergies:** the dyad self-organizes (unintentional in-phase coupling, p. 186–188). Fighters fall into rhythm with opponents; games can exploit this (break the opponent's rhythm = perturb the social synergy). This grounds `LANGUAGE.md`'s "Pace — the cadence at which one fighter forces the engagement."
  - **No mirror neurons needed** (Box 11.1): opponent-reading is *information detection*, not simulation. Keep coaching framed as "read the information," not "imagine yourself in their position."

### 2.12 Haptics / dynamic touch → grappling, grips, frames, weapons
- The book's haptics chapter (Ch. 8) is the theoretical basis for all **grappling/clinch** games. The information for touch is the **inertia tensor (I)** — resistance to rotation at the grasp point (p. 123–127). A fighter perceives the opponent's structure, base, and "off-balance-able-ness" through **what their grips/frames feel** (the inertial array), exactly as Fred perceives the racket's length and hit-with-able-ness blindfolded (p. 122–123).
- **Design:** Clinch/grappling games should make the **win condition about perceiving and exploiting felt information** — off-balancing via redirecting directional force (The Square's "redirect, don't just absorb" is precisely this, p. 124 of the book on rotational force). Cue athletes to attune to **pressure/resistance through the grips** (`key-concepts.md` already lists haptic "feeling submission opportunities" — upgrade to name the *inertial array / resistance-to-rotation* as the specific variable).
- **Forces and frames:** "Base down… so strike force doesn't rob your counters" (The Square) is the postural-synergy-supports-suprapostural-behavior principle (p. 149–151) — stable base is the support for offense; they are cooperative.

- **Repetition without repetition; variability for adaptability; degeneracy** — `environment-design.md` Principle 4, `key-concepts.md`. Bernstein p. 145; robot study p. 169–170; multirealizability p. 274.
- **Synergies as soft-assembled, goal-preserving** — `key-concepts.md` "Synergy Formation." Matches jaw-tug study p. 146.

These are model games.

### 3.2 Where we OVERSIMPLIFY or under-specify
- **"Individualize constraints" with no method.** `pitfalls.md` Pitfall 5 says match constraints to body type but never says *how*. **Correction:** scale by **critical π ratios** (reach/leg-length/shoulder-width), the book's body-scaling result (Warren, p. 79–81). This is the precise mechanism we are missing.
- **Generic "information sources" table.** `key-concepts.md` lists modalities but not the *specific invariants*. **Correction:** name **τ (time-to-contact), global optic flow, and the inertia tensor** as the variables that actually specify affordances (p. 82, 123–127, 165–166). "Reading" should mean "attuning to τ," not vaguely "seeing openings."
- **Dynamical-systems vocabulary is thin and slightly loose.** `theory.md` lists "attractors / control parameters / phase transitions" but the docs don't operationalize them, and don't distinguish **learning (birth of attractor)** from **adaptation (movement of attractor)** (p. 241), don't name **order vs. control parameters** cleanly, and don't frame skill as an **attractor layout** the coach reshapes. **Correction:** adopt §2.8 wholesale.
- **"Self-organization" definition is incomplete.** We say "spontaneous emergence" but omit the load-bearing requirements: **local rules + constant interaction + minimal intelligence + emergence at a larger scale** (p. 270–272). Add these to `key-concepts.md`.
- **Learning transfer is treated as a goal but not as *mode-specific and fragile*.** Our docs assume skills built in games transfer; the book shows transfer **fails across action modes** (crawling→walking p. 160; walking→throwing p. 206). **Correction:** add an explicit "non-transfer across action modes" warning and require per-mode learning paths (§2.10).
- **Perceiving-affordances-for-others is barely present.** `key-concepts.md` mentions "shared affordances" briefly, but the kendo two-preferred-distances finding, perceiving opponents' affordances, and social synergies (Ch. 11, p. 175–191) are absent from `mma-applications.md` — the single most MMA-relevant chapter is under-used. **Correction:** add §2.11.
- **Affective Learning Design is correct but under-theorized.** `environment-design.md` ALD is good; the book grounds *why* (emotion is nested in the animal-environment system, p. 57; affordances are perceived in/for context, p. 161). Keep, and cite mutuality.

### 3.3 Where we may MISUSE a term or risk it
- **"Skill as adaptation, not acquisition."** Correct (`key-concepts.md`, `theory.md`), but the book distinguishes **learning** (new attractor) from **adaptation** (moved attractor) (p. 241). We use "adaptation" loosely to mean both. **Tighten:** reserve "adaptation" for adjusting an existing skill to context; use "learning" for acquiring a new coordination pattern. Our two-stage model (coordinate → adapt) maps onto this if we are careful.
- **"Knowledge OF vs. knowledge ABOUT."** `theory.md` cites this from Renshaw. Blau & Wagman don't use these exact terms but the substance — direct perception is detection that needs no verbalizable representation; "knowledge of" the environment is educated through doing — is fully consistent (p. 82, 204). Safe to keep; just know its primary grounding is *direct perception + education of attention*, not a separate doctrine.
- **"Perception-action coupling" diagram with INTENTION mediating** (`key-concepts.md`). Fine, and the book supports it via **education of intention** (which affordance to go for; p. 204) — intention selects which information to detect. Keep.
- **Hristovski et al. affordance types** (strikeability etc.) — not in this book, but fully consistent with Gibson's affordance framework. No conflict; it's a legitimate domain extension.
- **`LANGUAGE.md` is betting/analysis-focused** (CLV, EV, Kelly) and largely orthogonal to the training theory. Its **game-naming convention** is sound. One enrichment: the "five things to narrate" (environment, roles, core constraint, win condition, terminal goal) should add a sixth — **the information the athlete must attune to** (τ, optic flow, inertial array, preferred distance) — since "the invariant that defines the game" (their phrase for core constraint) is, in the book's terms, literally about *what specifies the affordance.*

### 3.4 A subtle but important correction on causality
`theory.md` says "small changes can produce large effects (nonlinearity)" and "minor rule changes can revolutionize fighting styles." This is **correct but the book gives the mechanism**: a control parameter pushed past a **critical value** causes a **nonlinear phase transition / attractor death** (HKB, p. 250). Frame our nonlinearity claims as *control-parameter-driven attractor reorganization*, not vague butterfly-effect language. This makes our claim falsifiable and designable.

## 4. Rules for Our Logic Moving Forward

A numbered, practical rule set the team must apply when building games, constraints, and content. Each cites its grounding.

1. **The unit is the fighter-opponent-cage system — never the fighter alone.** Every game includes an adaptive opponent and the live environmental constraints (boundary, range). (p. 57; mutuality.)

2. **Constrain to afford; never prescribe.** A constraint reduces the option space so functional behavior *emerges* — "outlets every 12 feet," not "outlet here." If a rule forces one specific solution, it is over-constraining. (p. 271; Bernstein synergies p. 146.)

3. **Scale constraints by critical ratios, not absolutes.** Distances, target heights, apertures, and ranges should be set as ratios of the athlete's reach / leg length / shoulder width so different bodies face the *same relational problem*. This is how we "individualize." (Warren, p. 79–81; gap/aperture p. 84–86.)

4. **Name the information the athlete must attune to, per game.** Specify the invariant: **τ (time-to-contact)** for closing strikes/shots, **global optic flow** for self-motion/cage approach, **the inertial array (resistance-to-rotation)** for grips/clinch. "Reading" means attuning to a named invariant, not vaguely "seeing openings." (p. 82, 123–127, 165–166.)

5. **Reward the *taken opportunity* (affordance), not isolated cues.** Score the realized behavior (a clean counter, a completed off-balance), because an affordance is a *simultaneous multi-property fit* perceived in itself, not a single property. (Multiple-fit p. 77; Box 5.1 p. 87–88.)

6. **Reward economical/preferred solutions, and let athletes find their own transition points.** Perception is action-scaled to effort; skilled actors transition at a comfortable *preferred* boundary before their max. Phase-advance triggers should be the athlete's perceived readiness, not a coach's command. (p. 81, 88.)

7. **Never decompose the perception–action loop.** Simplify by scaling the *whole* task (slower, smaller, lighter, fewer options); never break perception off from action. Static unopposed drilling removes the very information the skill needs. (Task simplification p. 207; Möbius p. 192.)

8. **Let synergies soft-assemble to the goal; cue cooperation with free forces.** "Points for staying standing," not "you must sprawl." Cue base, suspension, redirecting directional force — capitalize on gravity/reactive forces ("controlled falling"). (Synergy/goal-preserving p. 144–146; forces for free p. 143–144.)

9. **Repeat the *problem*, not the *movement*.** Coordination stage: repeat the same problem to assemble the synergy. Mastery stage: repeat the *goal* across varied opponents/contexts. Variability is the feature. (Repetition without repetition p. 145; robot study p. 169–170.)

10. **Treat skill as an attractor layout the coach reshapes — and distinguish learning from adaptation.** *Learning* = birth of a new attractor (lots of exploration, expect instability). *Adaptation* = moving an existing attractor to a new context (fight-camp). Name your **control parameters** (speed, pressure, range, fatigue, # opponents) and your **order parameter** (what you measure). (Attractors p. 234–242; learning vs adaptation p. 241; control parameter p. 250.)

11. **Use noise/instability on purpose.** The "amber zone" is functional noise that lets a fighter escape a shallow habit and find a deeper, better pattern. Perturb working patterns and reward re-organization. (Functional noise p. 242–243; perturbation recovery p. 272–273.)

12. **Expect nonlinear switches under a control parameter — design for them.** Stance, rhythm, and gait reorganize abruptly (not smoothly) past a critical value; "minor rule changes revolutionize styles" because they cross a critical threshold. Design constraints to push toward beneficial transitions. (HKB p. 250; gaits p. 152–155.)

13. **Build learning paths as: educate intention → educate attention → calibrate.** Choose which affordance to go for, attune to the invariant that specifies it, then scale the response with feedback (reduce feedback over time). (Jacobs & Michaels p. 204–206.)

14. **Assume NO transfer across action modes; verify transfer empirically.** Reading τ for striking ≠ reading τ for takedowns; build separate per-mode paths and test. Phase-scaffold (striking → clinch → takedown) precisely because it re-educates attention at each new mode. (Crawling↛walking p. 160; walking-calibration↛throwing p. 206.)

15. **Design opponent-reading explicitly: perceive affordances for self AND opponent.** Reward recognizing what the *opponent's* position affords *them*. (p. 175.)

16. **Build distance games on two preferred ranges + a dead middle.** A near range affords offense, a far range affords defense, the middle affords neither — reward living in the advantageous range and avoiding neutral; experienced fighters spend more time in the offensive range. (Kendo, p. 188–189.)

17. **Coach by giving local rules and letting athletes adapt — not a blueprint.** "Give the contractors a list of rules… the houses will be far more adapted to their circumstances, and you will have to do a lot less work." The coach is an environment architect / rule-giver, not a choreographer. (Self-organization p. 284; Box 16.2 p. 285.)

18. **Train with the gear, in motion, with consequence.** Affordances are for the *animal-plus-object* system and change with gloves/fatigue/weapons; a moving fighter occupies more space; emotion is nested in the system (Affective Learning Design). (Animal-plus-object p. 86–87; dynamic constraints p. 86; mutuality/emotion p. 57.)

19. **Design affordances and information *into* the environment; prefer information over instruction.** Make desired behaviors perceptually obvious (live boundary, clear range markers) and manipulate information to compel behavior (cf. Chicago's optic-flow lines → 36% fewer crashes) rather than telling athletes what to do. (Designing with affordances p. 212–216; optic-flow lines p. 211.)

20. **Keep our vocabulary disciplined and grounded.** Maintain `LANGUAGE.md`'s controlled-vocabulary ethic; add a sixth narration input — *the information the athlete must attune to* — to game capture. Reserve "adaptation" for moved-attractor work and "learning" for new-attractor work. (Term discipline; learning vs adaptation p. 241.)
