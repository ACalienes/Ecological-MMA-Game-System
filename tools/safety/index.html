    Safety & Intensity Rules

    Coaching ToolNon-NegotiableApplies to Every Game

    Safety principles in this system are non-negotiable. They cannot be overridden for competitive pressure, athlete preference, or training efficiency. The game serves learning, never the other way around.

    Five Non-Negotiable Principles

    🦵
    No falling bodyweight onto legs

    Takedowns and transitions never drop bodyweight onto an opponent's legs or knees.This is the most common source of serious training injury, protect the knee at all costs.

    ⚖️
    Control precedes gravity

    All takedowns are created via off-balancing, not dumping. The base must be compromised before descent begins.If you have to force someone down, you haven't earned the takedown, and you're inviting a scramble injury.

    👆
    Strikes are informational

    Strikes in games are light and informational. They measure distance, create reactions, and test control, not cause damage.A strike's job in a game is to specify information, not to hurt a training partner.

    🔒
    Submissions are emergent

    Submissions (especially DNS) are defensive tools that emerge from constraints, not primary objectives to be hunted.No jumping guard, no sitting to finish, no chasing submissions as the main goal.

    🛑
    Coach intervention is mandatory

    The coach must stop play the moment the game stops serving its purpose.Intervention isn't optional supervision, it's a structural part of every game.

    FLAGGED FOR REVIEW

    The Submissions are emergent principle contains errors and needs revision. Content is provisional until corrected.

    When the Coach Must Stop Play

    • Structure degrades into scrambling chaos
    • Intensity escalates beyond appropriate levels
    • Any safety violation occurs
    • Learning value has disappeared

    Intensity Zones

    Match effort to development. These are learning environments, not competition simulations, quality reading beats maximal output at every level.

    🟢
    Beginner · 30–40%

    Focus on pattern recognition.Low intensity keeps the problem visible, the athlete is learning what to look for before learning to do it under fire.

    🟡
    Intermediate · 40–60%

    Add resistance and timing pressure.Enough live pressure to force adaptation, still controlled enough to keep structure intact.

    🟠
    Advanced · 60–75%

    Realistic problem-solving.Close to fight realism, but still inside the cap, the skill must hold under genuine resistance.

    🔴
    Maximum Cap · 80%

    Never exceed in training games.Even advanced athletes stay under 80% in ecological games, beyond it, learning gives way to survival and risk.

    Red Flags: Stop Immediately

    Brute force without techniqueIndicates the constraints are mis-set, the game is rewarding strength over skill. Memorized repetitions without adaptationThe game is too predictable, there's no live problem left to solve. Ego-driven intensity escalationA competitive mindset has taken over, the learning frame is gone. Panic responsesThe athlete is overwhelmed, the problem is too hard for their current level. Static grinding without progressNo learning is occurring, the exchange has stalled out.

    When red flags appear, stop the game and reassess:

    • Are the constraints appropriate?
    • Is intensity appropriate?
    • Does the athlete understand the problem?

    Safety Rules by Environment

    🟦
    Open Space

    No chasing collisions · no forward crashes · reset if exchanges become static.

    🧱
    Wall

    No grinding into the wall · no neck cranks · light strikes only.

    🔻
    Transitions (Wall → Ground)

    Control precedes gravity · no dumping bodyweight · no slamming · stop if descent becomes uncontrolled.

    🟫
    Ground

    No falling onto legs · DNS recognized and stopped before completion · no sustained suffocation.

    FLAGGED FOR REVIEW

    The Wall environment rules need revision.

    DNS in Training

    Defensive Neck Submissions (guillotines, front headlocks) occur when the attacker's head drops into a vulnerable position. DNS is not a safety concern, it's a natural consequence of poor head position, and the defender may capitalize with a choke.

    Playing to submission→DNS goes to finish
    DNS leads to ground, submission maintained→Continue to finish
    Any submission→Standard tap rules apply

    Intervention Language

    Coaches use clear, consistent commands so play stops the instant it needs to.

    🔁 "Reset"Return to the starting position. ❄️ "Freeze"Stop all movement immediately. 📉 "Check intensity"Reduce effort level. ⏱️ "Time"Round complete. 🚪 "Out"Exit the game, a safety concern has been raised.

    Athlete Responsibility

    • Respect tap signals immediately
    • Self-regulate intensity
    • Report any discomfort or injury
    • Prioritize partner safety over "winning"

    Culture of Safety

    The safest gyms are those where athletes actively protect each other, not just themselves.

    System evolution notice

    Safety rules may be expanded as new games or contexts are added. They expand, they never relax.
