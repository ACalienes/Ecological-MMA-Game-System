# GAME: [WIP] The Square — Close Defense to Takedown Progression

> **[WIP] — provisional.** Name and structure derived from the 2026-05-25 class recording, pending Alex's review on the platform. Drop the `[WIP]` tag (here, in the filename, and in cross-links from Tight Block / Close-Range Defense) once confirmed.

## GAME IDENTITY
- Problem: Defending and surviving in a confined space, then earning the right to counter, clinch, and finish
- Environment: Open Space → Confined Square (no exit)
- Stage: Access → Counter → Control

This is a **progression game**, not a single fixed game. It begins as close-range blocking defense inside a confined square and is layered live — one constraint at a time — until it becomes a full striking-to-takedown game. It is the practical, in-class expression that bridges **Tight Block**, **Close-Range Defense**, and **Pressure to Takedown**.

> **Provenance:** Derived from the class recording of 2026-05-25 (first audio-captured session). The phase structure below reflects how the game was actually scaled in real time, not an idealized level list.

---

## GOAL

This is an **asymmetric game** with distinct roles that switch on a win.

- Offense: Land significant strikes (and, in later phases, takedowns) within the square
- Defense: Survive using a tight shell, stay in the square, and — as the game opens up — counter, off-balance, and finish

The objective shifts by phase: **survival → defensive dominance → control**.

---

## ENTRY CONDITION
- Both players start at close quarters inside a marked square / rectangle (tape, mat lines, imagined boundary)
- Roles assigned; the winner of an exchange moves to (or stays on) defense per phase rules
- Reset to neutral close range after each scored exchange or boundary violation

---

## INVARIANTS
1. **Confined space — no footwork escape.** Both players stay in the square. *(Phase 1+)*
2. **Both feet leaving the square = automatic loss**, whoever you are. The boundary is live, like a cage edge. *(Phase 2+)*
3. No tying up. Hand controls are legal to nullify the opponent, but break fast — boxing rules.
4. Strikes are scaled to the partner; offense must allow the game to play (no teeing off).
5. One new variable is added at a time. Earlier constraints persist unless explicitly lifted.

---

## TASK FOCUS

### Defense
- Maintain a tight shell; absorb on arms/gloves, not on exposed targets
- **Keep eye contact on center mass** to read incoming strikes (see KEY LOGIC)
- **Base down — bend the knees, create suspension** so strike force doesn't knock you off balance and rob your counters
- Use hand controls to interrupt the opponent's boxing; redirect directional force rather than absorb it
- Stay conscious of where your feet are relative to the boundary

### Offense
- Land significant strikes in scaled combinations (start ≤3, escalate to 2-significant-impact requirement)
- **Land with the front two knuckles** for clean energy transfer
- In later phases, mix striking with leg attacks / double-under entries to finish

Key internal questions:
- "Can I see the shoulder and hip motion, or am I blind behind my gloves?"
- "Am I based and balanced, or getting jostled out of my counters?"
- "Where is the boundary right now?"

---

## LEGAL ACTIONS
Scales by phase (see LEVELS). Across the whole game:
- Strikes: Boxing throughout; kicks above the waist added at Phase 4
- Wrestling: Introduced Phase 3 (clinch off-balancing), expanded Phase 5 (leg attacks, takedowns)
- Submissions: Only top neck strangles / guillotines at Phase 5 — no arm bars or "getting crazy"

---

## KEY LOGIC (GAME-SPECIFIC)

### Read center mass, not the head
> "The head is completely irrelevant. There's nothing about the head that gives me information about the hands or the feet — and the eyes will lie to you."

Maintain eye contact on **center mass**. This keeps shoulder motion and hip motion both inside your field of view — "the little X." Shoulder tweak previews the punch line; hip motion previews power generation and kicks. Dropping the head ("ostrich in the sand") disconnects you from the incoming data, and eventually the opponent finds the hole.

### Base and suspension under fire
The damage isn't only the strike — incoming force **knocks the defender off balance**, which is what actually kills the counter. Bend the knees and create suspension, "like an ATV that bounces up and down" maintaining itself instead of getting rocked. A based defender can generate energy forward into a counter instead of constantly recovering balance.

### The boundary is the lesson
Confining the square removes the footwork-escape habit and forces the defender to solve the problem *in the pocket*. Making "both feet out = loss" explicit converts the boundary from scenery into a live constraint — the same awareness a fighter needs near the cage.

### Redirect, don't just absorb
When the offense abandons clean boxing to bully forward ("cheater McGeeter"), that pressure is the defender's opportunity: redirect the directional force to off-balance and win. Being pushed out is the defender's problem to solve, not a foul to call.

---

## WIN CONDITIONS

By phase (cumulative):

- **Offense wins** by landing the required number of significant strikes (1, escalating to 2 distinct impacts in a combination), or — later phases — by connecting a leg attack / double-under and finishing to the ground, or by pushing the defender's feet out of the square.
- **Defense wins** by surviving the round, by landing a clean counter (1 strike; later, a kick to the body), by off-balancing the offense in the clinch, or by establishing a top neck strangle.
- **Either player loses** the instant both feet leave the square.
- A caught kick = not successfully landed; the kicker is liable if it's used to push them out or take them down.

Round length: short, reset-driven exchanges; switch roles on a win.

---

## LEVELS

These are the live phases, added one at a time:

### Phase 1 — Confined Block + Hand Controls
- Offense: ≤3-punch combination, land 1 significant shot to win
- Defense: close blocking only — no parrying, no head movement, no footwork escape
- Hand controls legal to nullify; no tie-ups (break fast)

### Phase 2 — Counter + Live Boundary
- Defense may counter with **one** strike (interrupt the rhythm; keep defending, don't switch)
- **Both feet out of the square = loss**

### Phase 3 — Clinch Off-Balancing
- Defense can win by **off-balancing the opponent in the clinch** (redirect directional force)
- Offense now must land **two** significant strikes, and may enter on the legs / double-under body lock

### Phase 4 — Kick Counters
- Defense may counter with **kicks above the waist** (e.g., body kick after a block)
- A caught kick is not a score and leaves the kicker liable
- No kicks to head or legs — body only

### Phase 5 — Single-Leg Takedown Finish (new game)
- Start from a single-leg position; inside grip, alternating to outside
- Offense: get the opponent's hips to the ground (hands down / feet up = on track); no scrambling once hips land
- Defense: separate and get out, strike at any level, redirect-to-top, or finish with a top neck strangle / guillotine
- Back exposure = bonus

## ADVANCED EXPRESSIONS (COACH CONTEXT)

- The progression is built to **fill the class's collective deficiency** — "the games are designed to fill the deficiency in total of the class." Friction is the point: the game targets what the room is weak at and sharpens it.
- **Add one variable at a time.** When students asked for more counter tools early (shadow knee, clinch), the answer was a deliberate "no — keep it hands only" until the base skill held; grappling was introduced a phase later. Resist enriching the game before the current constraint has done its work.
- Watch for the **half-enjoying / half-struggling** signal ("it sucks to get punched in the face"). That blend is the desensitization working — students reported the value was exactly the clinch tolerance and eye-contact reading "that we don't normally work on."
- Everything ties back to the cage: the boundary, the base, the in-pocket defense all transfer directly to fighting close to the fence.

---

## SAFETY
- Contact limits: Light to moderate — felt but not damaging. Note that basing + front-two-knuckle contact makes punches stronger; scale accordingly.
- Stop conditions: Loss of control, caught-kick scrambles going wrong, boundary disputes, hips hitting the ground (game over — no scrambling).
- Coach intervention: Reset if the offense crowds and removes the defensive opportunity, or if tie-ups stall the action.
