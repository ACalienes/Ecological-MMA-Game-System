    Body Lock to Ground (WIP)

    Open Space · GrapplingBody Lock to Ground

    Provisional (WIP): invented 2026-06-15, re-validated on the mat 2026-06-20, pending final coach sign-off

    Built on the mat on 2026-06-15. Alex flagged it as a standard, "we're going to play that one a lot." It trains the standing back-and-body-lock grounding battle, the offensive mirror of Back Escape. Re-validated on 2026-06-20 (the R5 mat pass), which refined the defender's escape to a 50/50 Clinch reset and added a wall-proximity start. Awaiting a final pass before the WIP tag comes off.

    Open SpaceGrapplingOffensiveIntermediateGrounding Battle

    Put a standing opponent's hips on the ground from the body lock or the back, before they break the grip and turn.

    StartAttacker has a body lock or chest-to-back control; defender sets their posture first, inside a marked perimeter.
    →
    The GoalAttacker off-balances the hips to the ground; defender breaks back to the 50/50 clinch, or threatens the neck.
    →
    FinishHips down on top 1-2s → attacker · Break back to the 50/50 clinch or a neck strangle → defender · Out of bounds → loss.

    Don't hold and wait.
    The grip that waits, loses.

    Off-balance the instant it starts, side to side, until the hips touch down. Especially against someone stronger, move first.

    What to Read

    Attune to the defender's posts and weight shifts, where their base is loaded and which post is holding them up. Drag them to the weak side, take a post away, and the hips fall. Read the shifting base, not a memorized throw. When their hands go to the mat, they're no longer fighting your hands.

    The Starting Position

    PlayersTwo, one attacker (behind or around), one defender (held).
    PositionTwo starts: a body lock, or chest-to-back standing control. Defender sets overhooks / whizzer / posture before "go".
    BoundaryA marked perimeter, both stay inside. Start one body-length off the wall, the wall is live but not automatic.
    RolesAttacker grounds the hips; defender breaks free, off-balances out, or threatens the neck.
    Start & resetLock in tight, then "go"; reset on a grounding, a full separation, or a neck strangle.

    The Matchup

    🤼
    Attacker (Grounding)

    Trying to put one or both of the defender's hips on the ground and end on top, from the body lock or the back.

    Off-balance immediately, don't hold and wait. Use feet-to-feet contact, your head as a fulcrum, and side-to-side drags. From the body lock you may switch to a single leg. Get their hands to the mat, then they're posting, not fighting your grip.
    VS
    🛡️
    Defender (Surviving)

    Trying to break back to the 50/50 clinch, off-balance the attacker to break free, or threaten a top neck strangle.

    Set a whizzer or overhook before the start. Split the grip down the line, put a hip in as a blade so you're not square, then turn and face. Keep one hand back to stop the re-shot. A guillotine or front headlock is a live win.

    The Rules

    🎯 Attacker wins by hips down 1-2sOne or both of the defender's hips put on the ground with the attacker on top, held one to two seconds to clear a scramble. Knees, shoulders, and turtle do not count, the hips have to touch. 🐢 Turtle is not a winIf the defender drops to turtle, that is not a grounding. The attacker still needs the hips down. A belly-down grapevine with the hips flattened counts as hips down. ⛰️ Must end on topThe attacker has to finish with weight on top of the grounded hips. Ending underneath, even in a strong jiu-jitsu bottom position, does not score, the game rewards top control, not pulling guard. 🔁 Redemption shot on re-connectIf the defender breaks off and the attacker re-connects (for example re-shoots a single leg), it is on the defender to push all the way to a full separation. The attacker gets that redemption shot before the round is called for the defender. ⚖️ No falling body weightNo dropping your full weight on a downed partner. Off-balance and follow to top under control, this is a grounding game, not a slam. 🪢 Defender's neck strangle is liveA top neck strangle (guillotine or front headlock) that submits the attacker is a defender win, the same threat that keeps a real grounding attempt honest. 🤼 Defender resets to the 50/50 clinchThe defender's separation win is to break back to a neutral 50/50 over-under clinch, not just to flail free. Handing the defender the 50/50 as the escape route is deliberate, it incentivizes the attacker to keep pummeling and re-grounding rather than stalling on a stalemate grip. 🥊 Strikes on, GnP by permissionStrikes are live as a disincentivization tool. Use realistic strikes with space, not pitter-patter, by coach permission. Ground games train smashing, not grappling for its own sake. ⬛ Stay inside the perimeterPlay happens inside a marked perimeter, any shape. If a player leaves it, that player loses the round.

    How to Win

    Attacker Hips on the ground, on top, held 1-2s → attacker wins.One or both hips down with the attacker's weight on top, held one to two seconds to prove it past the scramble. A belly-down grapevine with flat hips counts; turtle, knees, and shoulders do not.
    Defender Break back to the 50/50 clinch, off-balance break, or a neck strangle → defender wins.Break the grip back to a neutral 50/50 over-under clinch, off-balance the attacker to force the break, or finish a top neck strangle (guillotine / front headlock). The redemption rule means a half-break that gets re-connected is not yet a win.
    Loss Leave the perimeter, or drop falling body weight → that player loses.Crossing the marked perimeter loses the round. Dropping full weight on a downed partner is a safety foul.

    The Levels

    1Body lock, off-balance onlyNo strikes, find the weak post.Start in a body lock, defender sets their posture first. Attacker works feet-to-feet and side-to-side off-balancing to put the hips down, no strikes. Finds the drag-to-the-weak-side mechanic.
    2Body lock, defender breaksGrounding vs separation.Same start, the defender now actively breaks grips and separates, and may switch to a single. The redemption shot is live. The grounding-versus-escape race becomes the task.
    3Chest-to-back startThe tougher position.Start from standing back control, chest-to-back. Harder for the attacker to finish, harder for the defender to turn. Remove a post, drag to the weak side, hips down. Defender hand-fights the front-headlock and looks to turn and face.
    4Add the neckDefender's strangle is live.From either start, the defender's top neck strangle (guillotine / front headlock) is now a win, the cost that punishes a reckless, posture-broken grounding attempt.
    5Full expressionRandom start, wall live, strikes on.Coach calls body lock or chest-to-back at random, strikes live, play starts one body-length off the wall. Lock in tight before "go", off-balance immediately, finish the hips down or break back to the 50/50.

    Recall Check

    Test yourself before moving on. Answer out loud, then reveal what good looks like.

    Q On offense, why is holding the grip and waiting the worst thing you can do?
    A static grip loses eventually, especially against someone stronger. Off-balance immediately, side to side, so the defender is always reacting and never gets to settle and break free.
    Q What does getting the defender's hands to the mat buy you?
    If their hands are posting to hold themselves up, they are no longer fighting your hands. A posted hand is a committed base you can drag away to put the hips down.
    Q Why lock in tight before "go" instead of starting loose?
    Like arm-wrestlers fighting for the grip before the start, you claim your strongest position first. Starting loose hands the defender a free chance to break. Unless there's a clear skill or power gap to scaffold, take all the claim you can.
    Q As the defender, how do you break the grip without getting taken straight back?
    Split the grip down the line (the cigar-cutter), put a hip in as a blade so you're not square and can't be dragged straight back, then turn and face, keeping one hand back to stop the re-shot.

    Go Deeper

    Task focus & coaching cues

    Each role's job

    🤼
    Attacker (Grounding)

    Lock in tight, off-balance immediately and continuously, feet-to-feet and head as a fulcrum, remove the weak-side post, get the hands to the mat, finish hips down on top.

    🛡️
    Defender (Surviving)

    Set a whizzer first, split the grip down the line, hip in as a blade, turn and face, keep one hand back against the re-shot, threaten the neck when the posture breaks.

    Coaching cues

    ⚡
    Move first, especially vs stronger

    Ask the attacker: "Did you off-balance on go, or hold and wait?" The grip that waits loses. Against a stronger partner, motion beats strength.

    🎚️
    Scaffold only on a gap

    Start locked into your strongest position. Only give a softer start when there's a real skill or power discrepancy worth scaffolding, otherwise take full claim.

    🔄
    The 50/50 reset shapes the game

    Handing the defender the 50/50 clinch as the escape incentivizes pummeling and re-grounding instead of a stalemate. The reward you give the defender shapes the behavior that emerges, that is the dial.

    Constraints-Led analysis

    Constraints → Affordances

    Hips-down win, 1-2s hold→A clean, observable grounding past the scramble
    Turtle is not a win, must end on top→Rewards real top control, not stalling or pulling guard
    Redemption shot on re-connect→Trains finishing the separation, not a half-escape
    Defender's neck strangle is live→Punishes a reckless, posture-broken grounding attempt
    Defender sets posture before "go"→A representative, fightable start, not a gifted back
    Defender's escape is the 50/50 clinch→A representative re-neutralization, and a reward that incentivizes pummeling over stalling

    Implements Representative Learning Design (Renshaw et al., 2019): the body-lock and chest-to-back starts model the two most common standing rear-control battles, and the hips-down win keeps the real goal, grounding the opponent, intact while the neck threat preserves the cost of overcommitting.

    What athletes read

    ✋
    Haptic

    Load through the grip and the defender's posts → which base is committed, which way to drag, when the grip is breaking.

    🧭
    Proprioceptive

    Own hips square or bladed → whether you can be dragged straight back, and when the turn-to-face is open.

    👁️
    Visual

    Hands going to the mat, a broken posture → the post to drag and the neck-strangle window.

    What we measure (order parameter)

    Whether the attacker off-balances the hips to the ground faster than the defender can break the grip and turn. Track groundings vs. full separations, and whether the attacker stays in motion instead of holding and waiting. The off-balance versus break-and-turn race is the order parameter; when the attacker reliably grounds the hips by moving first, the skill has formed.

    Representativeness

    Models: the standing back-and-body-lock battle, grounding a turtled or standing opponent and finishing on top, the exact problem after a clinch or back-take in MMA.

    Two starts: body lock + backhips down, on top, 1-2sstrikes by permission

    Offensive mirror of Back Escape; follows Open Space Takedown; the top finish feeds Ground Control and Mount Maintenance.

    Readiness to progress

    • Off-balances immediately, never holds and waits
    • Drags to the weak-side post to drop the hips
    • Gets the defender's hands to the mat
    • Finishes on top, not underneath

    Warning signs

    Holds the grip and waits Counts turtle or a knee-down as a finish Ends underneath and calls it a win Drops falling body weight

    Safety & related games

    🤝 Controlled grappling, no falling body weight 🛑 Tap early on neck strangles, slow finishes only 🔁 Reset on a grounding, a full separation, or a stall
