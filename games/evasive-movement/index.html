    Evasive Movement

    Open Space · StrikingEvasive Movement
    Open SpaceStrikingDefensiveIntermediateFootwork

    Prevent engagement entirely through footwork and ring generalship, "getting on your bike."

    StartTwo fighters at medium range inside a marked perimeter.
    →
    The GoalEvader keeps distance; pursuer closes it and corners them.
    →
    FinishKeep distance for the duration → switch · Establish range or corner the evader → reset.

    The best defense,
    is the fight that never starts.

    Avoid engagement through footwork and ring generalship, not by absorbing strikes. Win the space, don't get cornered.

    What to Read

    Attune to the rate the open space is closing (global optic flow) and the pursuer's preferred distance, far affords your escape, near affords their engagement. Read their angle of approach and weight commitment, plus the gap to the perimeter behind you, not their eyes. That information specifies where your space is and when to pivot vs. circle.

    The Starting Position

    PlayersTwo, one evader, one pursuer.
    RangeMedium range, outside engagement.
    BoundaryA marked perimeter, prevents endless retreat.
    RolesOne evader, one pursuer.
    Start & resetPursuer closes; reset when range is established or time elapses.

    The Matchup

    🦅
    Evader

    Trying to avoid engagement and maintain distance for the set duration, pure movement, no striking at Level 1.

    Keep moving, static defense doesn't count. Use lateral movement, angles, and pivots. Conserve energy, and above all don't get cornered against the perimeter.
    VS
    🥊
    Pursuer

    Trying to close distance, cut off angles, and herd the evader toward the perimeter.

    Actively close, not just walk forward. Cut angles, pressure without overcommitting, and herd toward the boundary. Recognize when the evader is trapped, and keep composure through the frustration.

    The Rules

    🦅 Evader must keep movingStatic defense doesn't count, the evader's whole job is movement. Standing and blocking is not a solution here. 🥊 Pursuer actively closesThe pursuer must genuinely close and cut angles, not just walk forward passively. This creates real pressure to move efficiently. 🚫 No striking at Level 1At Level 1 the evader cannot strike, pure evasion. Single counters are introduced only at Level 3. ⬛ A marked perimeter forces decisionsPlay happens inside a marked perimeter, any shape (ring, cage, taped lines). The boundary prevents infinite retreat and creates decision points. ⏱️ Win by duration or by corneringThe evader wins by keeping distance for a set time; the pursuer wins by establishing range or cornering. Clear objectives for both roles.

    How to Win

    Switch Keep distance for the duration → switch roles.The evader avoided engagement and kept the space open the whole round, ring generalship demonstrated, and earns the pursuing role.
    Reset Pursuer establishes range or corners → reset, same roles.The space closed: the pursuer cut the angle and trapped the evader against the perimeter, or closed to engagement. Begin again at medium range.
    Diagnostic Watch movement quality, angles and pivots, not gassing.Beyond the win, the coach reads movement quality: economy, use of angles, and composure under pursuit, not frantic circling or gassing.

    The Levels

    1Pure movementEvader can only move.Evader moves only, no strikes. Pursuer walks/jogs forward with pressure. Footwork fundamentals.
    2Add feintsRead real vs. fake.The pursuer can feint to test reactions; the evader must read real vs. fake. Movement under pressure.
    3Evader can counterSingle counters while moving.The evader can throw single counters while moving, punishing pursuer overcommitment. Movement + counter integration.
    4Full MMA expressionShot / clinch threat.The pursuer can shoot or clinch; the evader must manage all threats while moving. Evasion under MMA pressure.

    Recall Check

    Test yourself before moving on. Answer out loud, then reveal what good looks like.

    Q What are you reading to know when to pivot vs. circle?
    The rate the open space is closing (global optic flow), the pursuer's angle and weight commitment, and the gap to the perimeter behind you. Not their eyes.
    Q Why must the evader keep moving, with static defense not counting?
    The whole job is movement and ring generalship, winning the space, not absorbing strikes. Standing and blocking is not a solution here.
    Q Why does the marked perimeter exist if it limits the evader?
    It prevents endless retreat and forces decision points: evasion must be purposeful, and getting cornered against it is the loss.
    Q What is the pursuer actually trying to do?
    Not chase, but cut angles and herd the evader toward the boundary, closing the space without overcommitting until they are trapped.

    Go Deeper

    Task focus & coaching cues

    Each role's job

    🦅
    Evader

    Lateral movement and angles; distance management; ring awareness; time pivots; conserve energy.

    🥊
    Pursuer

    Cut off angles; pressure without overcommitting; herd toward the boundary; recognize the trap.

    Coaching cues

    🗺️
    Where is your space?

    Ask evaders: "Where is your space? Where is the wall?" This develops spatial awareness, the foundation of ring generalship.

    ✂️
    Where do you want them?

    Ask pursuers: "Where do you want them to go?" This develops cutting strategy over chasing.

    Constraints-Led analysis

    Constraints → Affordances

    Evader can only move (L1)→Forces development of movement solutions
    Pursuer actively closes→Creates real pressure to move efficiently
    Marked perimeter exists→Decision points and consequences for poor positioning
    Win by duration or cornering→Clear objectives for both roles
    Bounded space→Evasion must be purposeful, not endless running

    Develops ring generalship, the perception of spatial affordances, through the dyadic system interaction (Renshaw et al., 2019).

    What the players read

    👁️
    Visual

    Pursuer's angle and weight commitment; distance to the perimeter → escape direction, pivot vs. circle.

    🧭
    Proprioceptive

    Own balance and foot position → pivot and direction-change execution.

    🫁
    Fatigue

    Own fatigue level → energy-conservation decisions.

    What we measure (order parameter)

    Whether the open space is opening or closing over time, is the evader keeping distance and using angles, or is the gap collapsing toward the perimeter? Track distance kept, cornering events, and movement economy. When the evader consistently keeps the space open without gassing, the skill has formed.

    Representativeness

    Models: avoiding a pressure fighter while maintaining positioning and energy efficiency.

    Simplified: no strikes at L1pure pursuit earlyclear duration win

    Movement patterns transfer to any distance-management situation, leads into Pressure to Clinch when evasion reaches the boundary.

    Readiness to progress

    • Maintains distance for the duration consistently
    • Movement is economical, not gassing prematurely
    • Uses pivots and angles, not just circling
    • Stays calm under pursuit pressure
    • Can verbalize: "I saw them cutting, so I changed direction"

    Warning signs

    Gets cornered frequently Gasses quickly Only circles one direction Panics near the boundary

    Safety & related games

    🤝 Light contact on any counters 🛑 Stop on boundary collisions or lost composure 🔁 Coach resets if the evader is truly stuck
