# Sources & Theoretical Foundations

<p class="emma-glabel">Primary Source</p>

<p class="emma-hl">📖 <b>Renshaw, I., Davids, K., Newcombe, D., &amp; Roberts, W. (2019).</b> <i>The Constraints-Led Approach: Principles for Sports Coaching and Practice Design.</i> Routledge.</p>

This text provides the methodological framework for our entire system. Its four Environment Design Principles, Session Intention, Constrain to Afford, Representative Learning Design, Repetition Without Repetition, directly shape how every game is structured.

<p class="emma-glabel">Foundational Theorists</p>

??? quote "James J. Gibson (1904–1979): Ecological psychology, affordances, direct perception"

    <p class="emma-sub">Key work</p>

    <p class="emma-hl">Gibson, J.J. (1979). <i>The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception.</i> Houghton Mifflin.</p>

    <p class="emma-sub">Core ideas</p>

    <div class="emma-cards">
      <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">🌍</div><div class="emma-card__t">Affordances</div><p>The environment offers action opportunities relative to the organism's capabilities.</p></div>
      <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">👁️</div><div class="emma-card__t">Direct perception</div><p>Information specifies action possibilities without cognitive mediation.</p></div>
      <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">🔁</div><div class="emma-card__t">Perception-action coupling</div><p>Perceiving and acting form a continuous loop, not separate processes.</p></div>
    </div>

    <p class="emma-sub">How we use it</p>

    <ul class="emma-checklist">
      <li>Games are designed around affordances (what the environment invites)</li>
      <li>Athletes learn to perceive opportunities, not execute memorized techniques</li>
      <li>Training maintains the perception-action link (no isolated drilling)</li>
    </ul>

??? quote "Nikolai Bernstein (1896–1966): Motor coordination, degrees of freedom, dexterity"

    <p class="emma-sub">Key work</p>

    <p class="emma-hl">Bernstein, N.A. (1967). <i>The Co-ordination and Regulation of Movements.</i> Pergamon Press.</p>

    <p class="emma-sub">Core ideas</p>

    <div class="emma-cards">
      <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">🎚️</div><div class="emma-card__t">Degrees of freedom</div><p>The body's enormous movement possibilities must be organized.</p></div>
      <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">🧊</div><div class="emma-card__t">Freeze → Release → Exploit</div><p>Learners constrain movement, then progressively free it.</p></div>
      <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">🤹</div><div class="emma-card__t">Dexterity</div><p>Finding solutions that are correct, quick, rational, resourceful.</p></div>
      <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">🔄</div><div class="emma-card__t">Repetition without repetition</div><p>Repeat problem-solving, not identical movements.</p></div>
    </div>

    <p class="emma-sub">How we use it</p>

    <ul class="emma-checklist">
      <li>Games allow multiple solutions (dexterity, not technique compliance)</li>
      <li>Levels progress from constrained to open (freezing → exploiting)</li>
      <li>Variability is built into practice (repetition without repetition)</li>
    </ul>

??? quote "Scott Kelso (b. 1947): Coordination dynamics, self-organization, phase transitions"

    <p class="emma-sub">Key work</p>

    <p class="emma-hl">Kelso, J.A.S. (1995). <i>Dynamic Patterns: The Self-Organization of Brain and Behavior.</i> MIT Press.</p>

    <p class="emma-sub">Core ideas</p>

    <div class="emma-cards">
      <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">🌀</div><div class="emma-card__t">Self-organization</div><p>Coordinated patterns emerge spontaneously under constraints.</p></div>
      <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">🧲</div><div class="emma-card__t">Attractors</div><p>Stable coordination states behavior tends toward.</p></div>
      <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">⚡</div><div class="emma-card__t">Phase transitions</div><p>Shifts between patterns when control parameters change.</p></div>
      <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">📉</div><div class="emma-card__t">Instability precedes change</div><p>Variability increases before new patterns emerge.</p></div>
    </div>

    <p class="emma-sub">How we use it</p>

    <ul class="emma-checklist">
      <li>Techniques emerge from constraints, not instruction</li>
      <li>Games create conditions for self-organization</li>
      <li>Productive instability (amber zone) is necessary for learning</li>
    </ul>

??? quote "Karl Newell (b. 1945): Constraints model for motor development"

    <p class="emma-sub">Key work</p>

    <p class="emma-hl">Newell, K.M. (1986). Constraints on the development of coordination. In M.G. Wade &amp; H.T.A. Whiting (Eds.), <i>Motor Development in Children: Aspects of Coordination and Control</i> (pp. 341–360). Martinus Nijhoff.</p>

    <p class="emma-sub">Core ideas</p>

    <div class="emma-cards">
      <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">🧍</div><div class="emma-card__t">Individual</div><p>Organismic constraints unique to the athlete.</p></div>
      <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">🎯</div><div class="emma-card__t">Task</div><p>The rules and goals of the activity.</p></div>
      <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">🌳</div><div class="emma-card__t">Environmental</div><p>The surrounding physical and social context.</p></div>
    </div>

    <p class="emma-hl">Behavior emerges from the <b>interaction</b> of all three, not any single factor. Constraint manipulation guides learning without prescribing movement.</p>

    <p class="emma-sub">How we use it</p>

    <ul class="emma-checklist">
      <li>Every game manipulates specific constraints</li>
      <li>Rules (task constraints) shape what behaviors emerge</li>
      <li>Individual differences are respected (not one-size-fits-all)</li>
    </ul>

??? quote "Michael Turvey (b. 1942): Action systems, ecological realism"

    <p class="emma-sub">Key works</p>

    <p class="emma-hl">Turvey, M.T. (1990). Coordination. <i>American Psychologist, 45</i>(8), 938–953.<br>Turvey, M.T., &amp; Shaw, R.E. (1999). Ecological foundations of cognition. In R. Núñez &amp; W.J. Freeman (Eds.), <i>Reclaiming Cognition</i> (pp. 95–110). Imprint Academic.</p>

    <p class="emma-sub">Core ideas</p>

    <div class="emma-cards">
      <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">🧩</div><div class="emma-card__t">Intra-individual</div><p>Organizing body parts relative to each other.</p></div>
      <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">🤝</div><div class="emma-card__t">Inter-individual</div><p>Organizing the body relative to environment/opponents.</p></div>
      <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">🛠️</div><div class="emma-card__t">Action systems</div><p>Temporarily assembled, task-specific organizations.</p></div>
    </div>

    <p class="emma-sub">How we use it</p>

    <ul class="emma-checklist">
      <li>Games develop both types of coordination</li>
      <li>Combat sports require continuous inter-individual coordination (reading opponent)</li>
      <li>Synergies (movement solutions) are soft-assembled, not hardwired</li>
    </ul>
