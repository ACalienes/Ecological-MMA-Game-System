## Class structure (4 game blocks)

### Block 1: Warm-up, basic blocking defense (~0:00-1:30)
Close blocking / parrying vs a 4-strike-max combination. Existing Tight Block / Close-Range Defense territory, nothing new.

### Block 2: "Winning the Circle" wall-press game, 3 iterations (~1:36-40:00)
Asymmetric-weapons pressure game. **Offense:** lead hand + rear leg only; wins when defender's hip or back touches the wall (touch, not pin). **Defense:** rear hand + footwork; wins by level-change connection (one or both legs, under both arms) or staying mobile on the outside.

Live iteration sequence (this is the designer signal):

| Iter | Change | Why |
|---|---|---|
| v1 | Base rules, 8-min round | Students felt it was "dissonant... a lot of crashing" |
| v2 | Defense gains lead leg (teep) | "More defensive capability in terms of keeping somebody away" |
| v2b | Partner switch, same rules | Experiment; debrief surfaced "more feints" with fresh opponent, Alex: "I genuinely don't know why. I'm curious." |
| v3 | Defense win raised: connect AND destabilize (move partner's feet from their spot) | Threshold rule applied live; Alex: "still trying to figure out what's the best way to define it" |

**Naming candidate:** this is a *variant of Pressure to Wall*, not a new page (per the merge-not-multiply law). Suggest a variant block: "[WIP] Stalk and Corral" (Alex's verb: "stalk their prey... corral").

**Key coaching moment (22:29):** student got "shark eyes" for landing strikes and missed a free win (one push to the wall). Alex's articulation of the actual skill: "that ability to get somebody to the point where they are literally pressed to the wall, that's the skill that we're developing." Strong cue-language for the Pressure to Wall page.

### Block 3: Wall single-leg breakdown + difficulty ladder (~41:00-58:35)
Start shoulder-to-shoulder on wall. **Offense:** high single (grip under the knee, hiked high), wins by putting one/both of defender's hips on the ground "for a meaningful period" (threshold rule again; knees/shoulders don't count). **Defense:** wins by separating, reversing (ending on top), or threatening a top neck strangle (guillotine/ninja). **Strikes ON.**

Second round added a **4-level win-scaling ladder**, each win moves offense to a harder start:
1. High single (leg up) → 2. Body lock / double unders (defender gets whizzer) → 3. Regular single, leg down → 4. Low single, knees on ground.

**GnP directive enacted live (53:04):** "You are allowed to be meaner with your ground and pound striking in these games. I'm giving you permission. Use it as a disincentivization tool." This is the car-audio design law (ground games are GnP-first, not jiu-jitsu) operating in practice. Should become explicit rule text in ground game docs.

**Naming candidate:** variant family under Wall to Ground, or new page if the ladder is the identity: "[WIP] Wall Takedown Ladder." The self-scaling ladder mechanic is reusable across many games and may deserve its own FORMAT entry (like the positional-sparring format), not just a game.

### Block 4: NEW Alex-original game, invented on the spot (~58:43-68:14)
**"[WIP] Turtle Breakout"** (name TBD). Defense in turtle; offense standing, freestyle/Greco posture, palms on the small of the back, arms fully extended. Defense initiates ("go"). **Offense wins** by connecting hands around waist or any leg(s). **Defense wins** by standing, turning, facing, and staying away. **Redemption rule:** offense gets one final connection attempt during the escape; miss it and it's over.

Live designer observation (67:41): players went turtle → supine instead of exploding up. Alex on mic: "Maybe we need to constrain the rules so that they can't go supine. I'm not sure." → open design question for the doc.

Student insight in debrief: stop chasing the body lock, keep re-orienting hand connections, pin defender's hands to the mat first. Good "discoveries players make" material.

## Debrief coaching (69:00-72:06), reusable eco-pedagogy quotes

- **Pre-game planning habit:** "every time you hear the rules of the game, take 30 seconds and put a game plan to yourself: what are the things I can genuinely test to solve this problem."
