## The Metronomes on a Board

**What they did.** Several metronomes set ticking out of phase, placed on a board that rests on free-rolling cans. Left alone, they drift into synchrony. To break the synchrony, you don't adjust any metronome, you flip the cans so the board can no longer move and transmit vibration.

**What it proves.** The body is a *system, not a machine.* You change a system's behavior by **changing the constraint**, not its components, and the coordinating interaction (the moving board) is load-bearing; remove it and the pattern stops.

<p class="emma-hl"><b>Why it matters to us:</b> The whole constraints-led method in one image. To change a fighter's behavior, change the environment's constraints, don't try to re-wire the parts.</p>

*(Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, p. 61–62)*

## Warren's Stairs (π = 0.88)

**What they did.** Tall and short participants judged whether stairs of varying riser heights were climbable, and then climbed. Researchers plotted the boundary against absolute step height, then against the *ratio* of step height to leg length.

**What it proves.** Against absolute height the curves differ by body; against the **ratio** they collapse onto one critical value, **π = 0.88**, above which stepping is impossible, and the *perceived* boundary matched the biomechanical one. A second experiment found the *optimal* (least-effortful) step at a ratio of **0.26**, and people could perceive which stairs were most efficient. Affordances are **body-scaled** and **action-scaled.**

<p class="emma-hl"><b>Why it matters to us:</b> This is HOW we individualize constraints, by ratio of reach / leg length / shoulder width, not absolute numbers, so every body faces the same relational problem. And scoring should reward the economical solution, not just the possible one.</p>

*(Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, Warren, 1984, p. 78–81)*

## Bernstein's Blacksmiths

**What they did.** Bernstein filmed expert blacksmiths striking the same target repeatedly and tracked both their joint trajectories and the trajectory of the hammer head.

**What it proves.** The **joint trajectories varied** from strike to strike, but the **hammer-head trajectory stayed consistent.** A synergy produces *micro-level variability with macro-level consistency*, the system preserves the goal-relevant outcome, not a fixed set of movements.

<p class="emma-hl"><b>Why it matters to us:</b> Repetition without repetition is real and measurable. Don't chase identical reps; chase a consistent <i>outcome</i> assembled from varying movements.</p>

*(Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, p. 145)*

## The Jaw Tug

**What they did.** As a speaker produced a syllable, researchers mechanically perturbed the jaw mid-utterance and measured which articulators compensated.

**What it proves.** The compensation was **goal-specific:** for "b" the **lips** adjusted to still close the sound; for "z" the **tongue** adjusted. The synergy is self-correcting and recruits *whatever parts preserve the goal*, it is not a fixed muscle set.

<p class="emma-hl"><b>Why it matters to us:</b> "Points for staying standing, " not "you must sprawl." Constrain to the goal and let the synergy assemble and self-correct, the body will find a way you didn't prescribe.</p>

*(Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, Kelso, p. 146)*

## The Infant-Walking Robot Tournament

**What they did.** Two populations of walking robots were trained, one on **highly variable** infant walking paths, one on **regular** paths, then matched in a 4,000-game tournament.

**What it proves.** The variable-trained robots won **2,888 to 75.** Practicing across variable contexts produced far more adaptable, robust control than practicing a regular, repeated path.

<p class="emma-hl"><b>Why it matters to us:</b> The hardest evidence for variability. Once a pattern exists, vary opponents and contexts aggressively, that is what builds mastery, not clean repetition.</p>

*(Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, Box 10.2, p. 169–170)*

## Crawling ↛ Walking (Non-Transfer)

**What they did.** Infants who had learned to avoid slopes too steep to descend safely *while crawling* were tested again on the same slopes once they became *walkers.*

**What it proves.** They did **not** transfer the learning, they walked right off slopes they had safely refused to crawl down, "starting over from zero." The information for the affordance is *entirely different* across modes (different limbs, optic flow, base of support). Experience doesn't make babies "afraid of heights"; it makes them better at perceiving affordances *in context.*

<p class="emma-hl"><b>Why it matters to us:</b> Reading τ for striking ≠ reading τ for takedowns. Assume no transfer across action modes; build separate per-mode paths and verify transfer empirically.</p>

*(Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, p. 160–161)*

## Kendo's Two Distances

**What they did.** Researchers tracked interpersonal distance between kendo competitors over the course of bouts, comparing more- and less-experienced fighters.

**What it proves.** Competitors occupy **two preferred distances**, a **near** one that affords offense and a **far** one that affords defense, and **avoid the middle** that affords neither. More-experienced competitors spend *more* time at the near distance because they can move into striking position faster. Stepping toward or away is governed by **perceived affordances for striking the opponent.**

<p class="emma-hl"><b>Why it matters to us:</b> The gold-standard finding for distance management. Build distance games on two ranges + a dead middle; make "time in the offensive range" a progression marker.</p>

*(Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, Box 11.2, p. 188–189)*

## Chicago's Optic-Flow Lines

**What they did.** On a dangerous curve on Lake Shore Drive, transverse lines were painted at progressively *decreasing* spacing approaching the curve, increasing the rate of global optic flow as drivers advanced.

**What it proves.** The rising flow rate made drivers feel they were speeding up, so they slowed down, with **no conscious thought and no instruction.** Crashes fell by **36%.** Manipulating information compels behavior more reliably than telling people what to do.

<p class="emma-hl"><b>Why it matters to us:</b> Information design beats instruction. Make desired behavior perceptually obvious, a live boundary, clear range markers, and let the information do the coaching.</p>

*(Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, p. 211)*

## House-Building Robots

**What they did.** Wyss Institute robots were given **simple local rules** (and local information about neighboring blocks) rather than a master blueprint, then set loose to build structures.

**What it proves.** Whole houses **emerged** from local rules and constant interaction, self-organization with no central plan. The designer's lesson: give contractors a list of rules and let them adapt to local circumstances, and "the houses will be far more adapted… and you will have to do a lot less work."

<p class="emma-hl"><b>Why it matters to us:</b> The clearest articulation of the coach's role, a rule-giver, not a choreographer. Set the constraints; let the fighting style build itself.</p>

*(Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, Box 16.2, p. 285)*

## The Moving Room

**What they did.** Participants stood inside a room whose walls could be moved subtly while the floor stayed fixed.

**What it proves.** People **unconsciously swayed in sync** with the room, even being told to "stand still." Standing is "the prevention of falling, " a *balancing* task fed by **global optic flow**, not a static posture. Sway also *decreases* in directions that would disrupt a task and *increases* where it wouldn't, posture serves the suprapostural goal.

<p class="emma-hl"><b>Why it matters to us:</b> Base and posture are perceptually controlled and cooperative with offense, "base down so strike force doesn't rob your counters." Posture supports the behavior it carries; they're not in competition.</p>

*(Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, p. 147–151)*

## Prism Adaptation

**What they did.** Participants threw at a target while wearing prism glasses that optically shift the world sideways; their throws were tracked as they adjusted and then again after the glasses came off.

**What it proves.** They re-aimed to hit the target (and over-corrected the other way once the prisms were removed), an existing skill's attractor was **moved** to a new context. This is **adaptation** (movement of an attractor), distinct from **learning** (birth of a new attractor).

<p class="emma-hl"><b>Why it matters to us:</b> Name the difference. Teaching a genuinely new pattern is learning, expect instability, allow exploration. Tuning an existing skill to a new opponent is adaptation, that's fight-camp work.</p>

*(Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, p. 241)*
