<p class="emma-hl"><b>The attacker–defender dyad is the basic unit of combat.</b> Every game must include an adaptive opponent, keep reading coupled to reacting, and reward the fighter for perceiving what the <i>opponent's</i> position affords, not just executing their own offense.</p>

<p class="emma-glabel">Combat Sports Dyadic Systems</p>

<p class="emma-hl"><b>The attacker–defender dyad is the basic unit of analysis in combat sports.</b> It shows continuous co-adaptation, locally created information, constraint by distance/timing/position, and self-organization under competitive conditions.</p>

<div class="emma-warns">
  <span class="emma-warn emma-has-pop" tabindex="0">Never train one side of the dyad in isolation<span class="emma-pop">Even when focusing on developing the attacker, include a responding defender, the information lives in the interaction.</span></span>
</div>

<p class="emma-glabel">Perceiving Affordances for the Opponent</p>

The most MMA-relevant finding in ecological psychology is that **the same lawful principles that govern an animal–environment system govern an animal–animal–environment system** (Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, Ch. 11, p. 172–191). A fighter does not just perceive what *they* can do, they perceive, directly, what the *opponent* can do, because the same properties of the opponent that structure the energy *they* encounter also structure the energy *you* encounter. Combat is the book's own worked example.

<div class="emma-cards">
  <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">👁️</div><div class="emma-card__t">Affordances for self</div><p>"What can I do from here?", the lines, levels, and grips available to <em>you</em> right now.</p></div>
  <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">🎯</div><div class="emma-card__t">Affordances for the opponent</div><p>"What does <em>their</em> position afford <em>them</em>?", recognizing when the opponent is set to shoot, counter, or off-balance you (p. 175).</p></div>
</div>

<p class="emma-hl">Win conditions should reward reading the opponent's affordances, e.g., recognizing the instant the opponent's stance affords <b>them</b> a shot, not just executing your own offense.</p>

<p class="emma-glabel">Two Preferred Distances: and the Dead Middle</p>

The combat-sport gem: in kendo, competitors settle into **two preferred interpersonal distances** and **avoid the middle that affords neither** (Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, p. 188–189). A **near** range affords *offense*; a **far** range affords *defense*; the neutral middle affords nothing useful and is a trap. A fighter's choice to step in or out is driven by **perceived affordances for striking that opponent**. More-experienced competitors spend **more** time in the near (offensive) range because they can enter striking position faster.

<div class="emma-flow">
  <div class="emma-flow__row"><span class="emma-flow__from">Near range</span><span class="emma-flow__arr">→</span><span class="emma-flow__to">Affords <b>offense</b>, live in it; experienced fighters spend more time here</span></div>
  <div class="emma-flow__row"><span class="emma-flow__from">Dead middle</span><span class="emma-flow__arr">→</span><span class="emma-flow__to">Affords <b>neither</b>, a trap; move through it, don't dwell</span></div>
  <div class="emma-flow__row"><span class="emma-flow__from">Far range</span><span class="emma-flow__arr">→</span><span class="emma-flow__to">Affords <b>defense</b>, reset and read from here</span></div>
</div>

<p class="emma-hl">This is the primary-theory grounding for <b>distance management</b> as "the substrate underneath striking output." Build distance games on <b>two preferred ranges + a dead middle</b>: reward fighters for living in their advantageous range and avoiding the neutral zone. Make "more time in the offensive range" a <b>progression marker</b>.</p>

<p class="emma-glabel">Reading Movement that Shares Force Dynamics</p>

Perceiving an opponent's affordances is **learned by watching them move**, but only movement that *shares force dynamics with the threat* is informative (Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, p. 180–181). Watching someone *walk* informs their jump-and-reach ability (both need vertical leg forces); watching them *twist* does not. Domain-specific: basketball players read jump-and-reach affordances for others better than non-players, but are *no better* at sitting/standing-reach affordances, experience tunes perception of the *specific* affordances of the sport.

<div class="emma-cards">
  <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">⬇️</div><div class="emma-card__t">Level change ↔ shot</div><p>A weight drop shares force dynamics with the takedown, it specifies "shoot-able." Tape study and live reading should focus here.</p></div>
  <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">⚖️</div><div class="emma-card__t">Weight shift ↔ strike</div><p>Loading a side specifies the strike it powers. Read the load, not the hand.</p></div>
  <div class="emma-card"><div class="emma-card__i">🚫</div><div class="emma-card__t">Irrelevant motion</div><p>Feints that <em>don't</em> share force dynamics with a real threat carry no affordance information, train athletes to discount them.</p></div>
</div>

<p class="emma-glabel">Social Synergies: Break the Rhythm</p>

The two-fighter dyad **self-organizes into a synergy that spans both people**, the same in-phase/anti-phase dynamics that govern a single body (Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, p. 186–188). People rocking in chairs synchronize *unintentionally* when they can see each other; people walking together fall into a shared gait. Fighters fall into rhythm with an opponent the same way.

<p class="emma-hl">A game can exploit this: <b>breaking the opponent's rhythm = perturbing the social synergy</b>. This grounds <b>pace</b>, "the cadence at which one fighter forces the engagement." Reward the fighter who imposes their cadence and disrupts the opponent's.</p>

??? note "No mirror neurons required"
    Opponent-reading is <b>information detection</b>, not mental simulation (Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, Box 11.1, p. 177–178). Coach it as <i>"read the information the opponent's movement gives off, "</i> never <i>"imagine yourself in their position."</i> See the <a href="../../ecological-psychology/">Ecological Psychology</a> section.

<p class="emma-glabel">MMA Game Design Principles</p>

<div class="emma-cards">
  <div class="emma-card emma-has-pop" tabindex="0"><div class="emma-card__i">🥊</div><div class="emma-card__t">1 · Include the opponent</div><p>Every game has an adaptive opponent, not static targets.<span class="emma-pop"><b>Less effective → More effective:</b> heavy bag → partner with pads who reacts · solo drilling → positional sparring · choreographed sequences → constrained live rounds.</span></p></div>
  <div class="emma-card emma-has-pop" tabindex="0"><div class="emma-card__i">🔗</div><div class="emma-card__t">2 · Maintain coupling</div><p>Always reading real information and responding, never just executing.<span class="emma-pop"><b>Decoupled → Coupled:</b> shadow boxing w/o target → with reactive partner · mitts with called combos → mitts with visual cues · submission on compliant partner → with resistance.</span></p></div>
  <div class="emma-card emma-has-pop" tabindex="0"><div class="emma-card__i">🌡️</div><div class="emma-card__t">3 · Right challenge level</div><p>Match the variability zone to the learning goal.<span class="emma-pop"><b>Green:</b> new skill, confidence, limited-technique sparring, light pace. <b>Amber:</b> refinement, adaptation, full-technique sparring, moderate pace. <b>Red (sparingly):</b> testing limits, high-intensity sparring near competition.</span></p></div>
  <div class="emma-card emma-has-pop" tabindex="0"><div class="emma-card__i">🧩</div><div class="emma-card__t">4 · Allow multiple solutions</div><p>Don't prescribe a single "correct" technique.<span class="emma-pop"><b>Bad:</b> "When they shoot, you must sprawl." <b>Good:</b> "Defend the takedown, points for staying standing." The second allows sprawl, underhook, lateral movement, or any functional solution.</span></p></div>
  <div class="emma-card emma-has-pop" tabindex="0"><div class="emma-card__i">♻️</div><div class="emma-card__t">5 · Build degeneracy</div><p>Multiple ways to achieve goals, so fighters aren't stuck when one option is gone.<span class="emma-pop"><b>Takedown game:</b> train with wrestlers (counter-wrestling emerges), judoka (anti-clinch emerges), strikers (level-change emerges). Varied opponents → varied solutions → robust skill.</span></p></div>
</div>

<p class="emma-glabel">Priority Principles for MMA Training</p>

<ol class="emma-checklist">
  <li><b>Maintain perception-action coupling</b>, never separate reading from reacting</li>
  <li><b>Include live opponents</b>, pads and bags have limited transfer</li>
  <li><b>Vary opponents</b>, different sizes, styles, skill levels</li>
  <li><b>Create representative pressure</b>, emotional intensity matters</li>
  <li><b>Allow self-organization</b>, don't over-prescribe technique</li>
  <li><b>Embrace productive instability</b>, struggle leads to growth</li>
  <li><b>Know your fighters</b>, individual constraints matter most</li>
</ol>
