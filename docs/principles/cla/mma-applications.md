## Combat Sports Dyadic Systems

**Key Concept:** The attacker-defender dyad is the basic unit of analysis in combat sports.

**Properties:**

- Continuous co-adaptation
- Locally created information
- Constrained by distance, timing, position
- Self-organizing under competitive conditions

**Implication:** Never train one side of the dyad in isolation. Even when focusing on developing the attacker, include a responding defender.

## Perceiving Affordances for the Opponent

The most MMA-relevant finding in ecological psychology is that **the same lawful principles that govern an animal–environment system govern an animal–animal–environment system** (Blau & Wagman, 2022 — Ch. 11, p. 172–191). A fighter does not just perceive what *they* can do — they perceive, directly, what the *opponent* can do, because the same properties of the opponent that structure the energy *they* encounter also structure the energy *you* encounter. Combat is the book's own worked example.

<p class="emma-hl">Win conditions should reward reading the opponent's affordances — e.g., recognizing the instant the opponent's stance affords <b>them</b> a shot — not just executing your own offense.</p>

### Two preferred distances — and the dead middle

The combat-sport gem: in kendo, competitors settle into **two preferred interpersonal distances** and **avoid the middle that affords neither** (Blau & Wagman, 2022 — p. 188–189). A **near** range affords *offense*; a **far** range affords *defense*; the neutral middle affords nothing useful and is a trap. A fighter's choice to step in or out is driven by **perceived affordances for striking that opponent**. More-experienced competitors spend **more** time in the near (offensive) range because they can enter striking position faster.

<p class="emma-glabel">The distance map</p>

<div class="emma-flow">
  <div class="emma-flow__row"><span class="emma-flow__from">Near range</span><span class="emma-flow__arr">→</span><span class="emma-flow__to">Affords <b>offense</b> — live in it; experienced fighters spend more time here</span></div>
  <div class="emma-flow__row"><span class="emma-flow__from">Dead middle</span><span class="emma-flow__arr">→</span><span class="emma-flow__to">Affords <b>neither</b> — a trap; move through it, don't dwell</span></div>
  <div class="emma-flow__row"><span class="emma-flow__from">Far range</span><span class="emma-flow__arr">→</span><span class="emma-flow__to">Affords <b>defense</b> — reset and read from here</span></div>
</div>

<p class="emma-hl">This is the primary-theory grounding for <b>distance management</b> as "the substrate underneath striking output." Build distance games on <b>two preferred ranges + a dead middle</b>: reward fighters for living in their advantageous range and avoiding the neutral zone. Make "more time in the offensive range" a <b>progression marker</b>.</p>

### Reading movement that shares force dynamics with the threat

Perceiving an opponent's affordances is **learned by watching them move** — but only movement that *shares force dynamics with the threat* is informative (Blau & Wagman, 2022 — p. 180–181). Watching someone *walk* informs their jump-and-reach ability (both need vertical leg forces); watching them *twist* does not. Domain-specific: basketball players read jump-and-reach affordances for others better than non-players, but are *no better* at sitting/standing-reach affordances — experience tunes perception of the *specific* affordances of the sport.

### Social synergies — break the rhythm

The two-fighter dyad **self-organizes into a synergy that spans both people** — the same in-phase/anti-phase dynamics that govern a single body (Blau & Wagman, 2022 — p. 186–188). People rocking in chairs synchronize *unintentionally* when they can see each other; people walking together fall into a shared gait. Fighters fall into rhythm with an opponent the same way.

<p class="emma-hl">A game can exploit this: <b>breaking the opponent's rhythm = perturbing the social synergy</b>. This grounds <b>pace</b> — "the cadence at which one fighter forces the engagement." Reward the fighter who imposes their cadence and disrupts the opponent's.</p>

!!! note "No mirror neurons required"
    Opponent-reading is **information detection**, not mental simulation (Blau & Wagman, 2022 — Box 11.1, p. 177–178). Coach it as *"read the information the opponent's movement gives off,"* never *"imagine yourself in their position."* See the [Ecological Psychology](../ecological-psychology/index.md) section.

## Designing MMA Training with CLA

### Example: Developing Takedown Defense

**Traditional Approach (Problematic):**

1. Drill sprawl technique repeatedly
2. Add static partner
3. Add moving partner
4. Hope it transfers

**Problems:**

- Separates perception from action (no real attack to read)
- No variability in attack timing, angle, setup
- Defender not learning to read—just executing on cue
- Low transfer to live situations

---

**CLA Approach (Preferred):**

1. Create environment with takedown threat
2. Allow self-organization of defensive solutions
3. Vary attacker timing, angles, setups
4. Include striking context (representative)
5. Adjust threat level (variability zones)

**Why It Works:**

- Perception-action coupling maintained
- Multiple defensive solutions can emerge
- Attacker variability builds defender adaptability
- Striking context ensures transfer to MMA (not just wrestling)

---

### Example: Developing Counter-Striking

**Session Intention:** Improve counter-striking off opponent attacks

**Constrain to Afford:**

- Partner must initiate attacks (constraint on partner)
- Reward system for successful counters
- Limit fighter's initiating options

**Representative Design:**

- Include realistic attack setups
- Maintain emotional intensity
- Keep perception-action coupled

**Repetition Without Repetition:**

- Vary partner attack selection
- Change timing and distance
- Alter angles and combinations

---

### Example: Developing Submission Defense

**CLA Game Design:**

**Constraint Setup:**

- Attacker starts with submission partially locked
- Defender must escape before completion
- Reset on escape OR submission

**Why This Works:**

- Defender perceives real submission threat (not simulated)
- Time pressure creates representative stress
- Multiple escape solutions can emerge
- Attacker learns finishing; defender learns surviving

**Progressions:**

1. Start with submission 50% locked (easier escape)
2. Progress to 75% locked (harder escape)
3. Add preceding position (escape from position + submission threat)
4. Full positional sparring with submission awareness

## MMA Game Design Principles

### 1. Include the Opponent

Every game should have an adaptive opponent, not static targets or predetermined patterns.

| Less Effective | More Effective |
|---------------|----------------|
| Heavy bag work | Partner with pads who reacts |
| Solo drilling | Positional sparring |
| Choreographed sequences | Constrained live rounds |

---

### 2. Maintain Perception-Action Coupling

The fighter should always be reading real information and responding—never just executing movements.

| Decoupled (Avoid) | Coupled (Use) |
|-------------------|---------------|
| Shadow boxing without target | Shadow boxing with reactive partner |
| Mitt work with called combinations | Mitt work with visual cues |
| Submission drilling on compliant partner | Submission training with resistance |

---

### 3. Create Appropriate Challenge Level

Match the variability zone to the learning goal.

| Zone | When to Use | MMA Example |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Green** | New skill, confidence building | Limited technique sparring at light pace |
| **Amber** | Skill refinement, adaptation | Full technique sparring at moderate pace |
| **Red** | Testing limits (use sparingly) | High-intensity sparring approaching competition |

---

### 4. Allow Multiple Solutions

Don't prescribe single "correct" techniques. Create constraints that allow various functional solutions to emerge.

**Bad:** "When they shoot, you must sprawl"
**Good:** "Defend the takedown—points for staying standing"

The second version allows sprawl, underhook, lateral movement, or any other functional solution.

---

### 5. Build Degeneracy

Ensure fighters have multiple ways to achieve goals, so they're not stuck when one option is unavailable.

**Example:** Takedown game

- Train with wrestlers (counter-wrestling solutions emerge)
- Train with judoka (anti-clinch solutions emerge)
- Train with strikers (level-change solutions emerge)

Varied opponents → varied solutions → robust skill.

---

## Priority Principles for MMA Training

1. **Maintain perception-action coupling** - Never separate reading from reacting
2. **Include live opponents** - Pads and bags have limited transfer
3. **Vary opponents** - Different sizes, styles, skill levels
4. **Create representative pressure** - Emotional intensity matters
5. **Allow self-organization** - Don't over-prescribe technique
6. **Embrace productive instability** - Struggle leads to growth
7. **Know your fighters** - Individual constraints matter most
