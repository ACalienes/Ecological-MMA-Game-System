# Session Design Framework

<div class="emma-meta"><span>CLA Methodology</span><span>GROW Model</span><span>Two Dials</span><span>Periodization</span></div>

<p style="font-size:.8rem;color:var(--fog-dim);margin:-.6rem 0 1.4rem;">← <a href="../environment-design/">Environment Design</a> · <a href="../index/">CLA Index</a> · <a href="../mma-applications/">MMA Applications</a> →</p>

<p class="emma-hl"><b>A session is a designed environment, not a list of drills.</b> You set a goal, read where the athlete is, choose constraints to bridge the gap, then prepare to deliver it, and you scale difficulty with two independent dials, not one.</p>

??? abstract "Sources"
    The GROW model and session planning frameworks are adapted from Renshaw et al. (2019). For complete bibliography, see <a href="../../../reference/sources/">Sources &amp; Theoretical Foundations</a>.

<p class="emma-glabel">Session Flow Pattern</p>

<div class="emma-flow">
  <div class="emma-flow__row"><span class="emma-flow__from">Green Zone</span><span class="emma-flow__arr">→</span><span class="emma-flow__to"><b>Confidence</b>, build, assess baseline; athletes arrive in varied states, green calibrates them</span></div>
  <div class="emma-flow__row"><span class="emma-flow__from">Amber Zone</span><span class="emma-flow__arr">→</span><span class="emma-flow__to"><b>Challenge</b>, the productive instability needed for learning</span></div>
  <div class="emma-flow__row"><span class="emma-flow__from">Green Zone</span><span class="emma-flow__arr">→</span><span class="emma-flow__to"><b>Consolidation</b>, lock in the pattern, end the session positively</span></div>
</div>

??? info "Why this pattern: functional noise, and hysteresis"
    The "productive instability" of the amber zone is what ecological psychology calls <b>functional noise</b> (Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, p. 242–243). Noise is not error: it constantly perturbs the system and is precisely what lets the "ball" escape a <i>shallow, local</i> attractor (a comfortable-but-suboptimal habit) and settle into a <i>deeper, better</i> one. In one study, <i>added noise made participants more likely to discover the efficient strategy</i> (p. 243). The amber zone is therefore deliberate, useful perturbation, not chaos to be avoided.

    The <b>GREEN → AMBER → GREEN</b> flow also respects <b>hysteresis</b> (p. 250): a system's current state depends on its <i>history/trajectory</i>, not just present conditions. A pattern entered under amber-zone pressure persists, so the order in which you pass through the zones matters, you cannot treat a session as path-independent. (See the <a href="../../ecological-psychology/index/">Ecological Psychology</a> section.)

<p class="emma-glabel">Scaling Difficulty: The Two Dials</p>

<div class="emma-cards">
  <div class="emma-card emma-has-pop" tabindex="0"><div class="emma-card__i">📡</div><div class="emma-card__t">Representativeness Dial (RLD)</div><p>1 → 10. Low = modified, simplified. High = full competition.<span class="emma-pop">How closely practice samples the information of the competition environment. Turn it up to demand more competition-like reading and pressure.</span></p></div>
  <div class="emma-card emma-has-pop" tabindex="0"><div class="emma-card__i">🌡️</div><div class="emma-card__t">Variability Dial</div><p>Green (stable/predictable) → Amber (productive instability) → Red (too much chaos).<span class="emma-pop">How much instability is in the environment. Amber is the target for learning; red overwhelms.</span></p></div>
</div>

<p class="emma-hl"><b>Use them together:</b> High RLD + Amber Zone = maximum learning transfer. Low RLD + Green Zone = safe exploration for beginners.</p>

??? note "The two dials are control parameters"
    In dynamical-systems terms, the representativeness dial and the variability dial are <b>control parameters</b>, variables that, when turned, reorganize the fighter's <b>attractor layout</b> (Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, p. 250). Turning them does not dictate the new behavior; it changes which behaviors are stable. Other control parameters you turn in a session include speed, pressure, range, fatigue, and number of opponents. Expect that pushing one <i>past a critical value</i> produces an abrupt, <b>nonlinear</b> reorganization (a stance or rhythm suddenly switches) rather than a smooth change, design for that switch rather than being surprised by it. (See <a href="../theory/">Theory</a> and the <a href="../../ecological-psychology/index/">Ecological Psychology</a> section.)

<p class="emma-glabel">Constraint Manipulation During Sessions</p>

<div class="emma-cards">
  <div class="emma-card emma-has-pop" tabindex="0"><div class="emma-card__i">🔒</div><div class="emma-card__t">When to tighten</div><p>Channel and contain.<span class="emma-pop">Athletes lost or frustrated (too much chaos) · need to channel exploration in a specific direction · building confidence after failure · introducing a new concept.</span></p></div>
  <div class="emma-card emma-has-pop" tabindex="0"><div class="emma-card__i">🔓</div><div class="emma-card__t">When to loosen</div><p>Open and test.<span class="emma-pop">Athletes succeeding too easily · patterns becoming rigid · need to test adaptability · building toward competition demands.</span></p></div>
</div>

<div class="emma-flow">
  <div class="emma-flow__row"><span class="emma-flow__from">High success, low effort</span><span class="emma-flow__arr">→</span><span class="emma-flow__to"><b>Too easy</b>, increase challenge (loosen / add constraints)</span></div>

  <div class="emma-flow__row"><span class="emma-flow__from">Rigid, repetitive patterns</span><span class="emma-flow__arr">→</span><span class="emma-flow__to"><b>System capture</b>, increase variability</span></div>
</div>

<p class="emma-glabel">The Four Session Types</p>

<div class="emma-cards">
  <div class="emma-card emma-has-pop" tabindex="0"><div class="emma-card__i">🌱</div><div class="emma-card__t">Learning</div><p>Developing new coordination patterns.<span class="emma-pop">Lower RLD (modified) · Green→Amber zones · higher acceptance of failure · exploration encouraged · coach more active in design.</span></p></div>
  <div class="emma-card emma-has-pop" tabindex="0"><div class="emma-card__i">🔧</div><div class="emma-card__t">Adapting</div><p>Refining and stress-testing patterns.<span class="emma-pop">Moderate→High RLD · amber-zone emphasis · varied conditions · problem-solving required · coach observes and adjusts.</span></p></div>
  <div class="emma-card emma-has-pop" tabindex="0"><div class="emma-card__i">🏆</div><div class="emma-card__t">Performing</div><p>Executing under representative pressure.<span class="emma-pop">High RLD (competition simulation) · Green→Amber zones · success orientation · full intensity · coach minimal intervention.</span></p></div>
  <div class="emma-card emma-has-pop" tabindex="0"><div class="emma-card__i">🎚️</div><div class="emma-card__t">Attuning</div><p>Calibrating to a specific environment/opponent.<span class="emma-pop">Very high RLD (specific simulation) · green zone · familiarization · confidence building · used pre-competition.</span></p></div>
</div>

<p class="emma-glabel">Session Debrief: Five Questions</p>

<ol class="emma-checklist">
  <li><b>What were the goals and expectations?</b> Intended affordances · success criteria.</li>
  <li><b>What actually happened?</b> Design effectiveness · coaching delivery · learning observed.</li>
  <li><b>What went well and why?</b> Learning-design successes · instruction/constraint effectiveness.</li>
  <li><b>What can be improved?</b> Design modifications · constraint adjustments.</li>
  <li><b>What are the key takeaways?</b> 3 things to continue · 3 things to change.</li>
</ol>

<p class="emma-glabel">Integration with Periodization</p>

<div class="emma-stepper">
  <div class="emma-step emma-has-pop" tabindex="0"><span class="emma-step__n">1</span><b>Off-Season</b>Lower RLD · higher variability · exploration, new skills.<span class="emma-pop emma-pop--l"><b>Attractor op:</b> Learning, <i>birth</i> of new attractors. Expect exploration and temporary instability.</span></div>
  <div class="emma-step emma-has-pop" tabindex="0"><span class="emma-step__n">2</span><b>Pre-Camp</b>Moderate RLD · moderate variability · building specific patterns.<span class="emma-pop emma-pop--l"><b>Attractor op:</b> Learning → adaptation transition.</span></div>
  <div class="emma-step emma-has-pop" tabindex="0"><span class="emma-step__n">3</span><b>Fight Camp</b>Higher RLD · lower variability · refining for the opponent.<span class="emma-pop emma-pop--l"><b>Attractor op:</b> Adaptation, <i>moving</i> an existing attractor to this opponent's context.</span></div>
  <div class="emma-step emma-has-pop" tabindex="0"><span class="emma-step__n">4</span><b>Fight Week</b>Highest RLD · lowest variability · execution, confidence.<span class="emma-pop emma-pop--l"><b>Attractor op:</b> Adaptation, stabilize the moved attractor.</span></div>
</div>

??? note "Learning vs. adaptation across the cycle"
    Periodization maps cleanly onto the dynamical distinction (Blau &amp; Wagman, 2022, p. 241). Off-season is <b>learning</b>, the <i>birth of a new attractor</i>, so expect exploration and temporary instability. Fight camp is <b>adaptation</b>, the <i>movement of an existing attractor</i> to fit a specific opponent, not the acquisition of something new. Reserve the words accordingly: do not call fight-camp refinement "learning, " and do not expect off-season instability during fight week. (See <a href="../theory/">Theory</a>.)
