    FAQ & Troubleshooting

    ReferenceCoach & Athlete Q&ATroubleshooting

    Common questions and fixes for coaches and athletes using this system. Tap any question to open the answer.

    General Questions

    What is the ecological approach?

    The ecological approach to motor learning rests on four pillars:

    🧩
    Constraints-led

    Use rules and boundaries to shape behavior.

    🎯
    Representative design

    Training mirrors the demands of competition.

    🌱
    Emergence

    Skills emerge from practice, not just instruction.

    🔍
    Exploration

    Athletes discover solutions through guided play.

    Games in this system are designed to create learning conditions, not to prescribe techniques.

    How is this different from traditional drilling?

    Coach prescribes technique→Athlete discovers solutions
    Repetition of fixed movements→Exploration of possibilities
    Compliance-focused→Problem-solving focused
    Isolated from context→Representative of real fighting

    Do I need to teach technique separately?

    Technique instruction can complement games, but:

    • Games come first, they create the "why"
    • Technique emerges from game play
    • Explicit instruction fills gaps, it doesn't replace discovery
    • Over-coaching can reduce learning

    Full MMA Expression

    What is Full MMA Expression?

    Full MMA Expression is the highest level of any game, where cross-domain threats are added while keeping the game's core focus.

    Striking games→Add grappling threats (shoot, clinch)
    Wrestling games→Add striking threats

    See: Full MMA Expression Concept

    When should athletes attempt Full MMA Expression levels?

    Only after they're proficient at lower levels. Don't rush it, the skill must be solid before adding complications.

    Signs of readiness:

    • Consistent success at levels 1–4
    • Primary skill feels automatic
    • Ready for additional cognitive load
    • Seeking realistic pressure

    How do I scale Full MMA Expression appropriately?

    Start conservative and build up:

    1Low intensityIntroduce the threat gently.
    2Announce it"I'm going to add a clinch option now."
    3Increase realismGradually ramp the threat up.
    4Pull back if neededRegress if the primary skill breaks down.

    Do all games need Full MMA Expression?

    Every game has a Full MMA Expression level documented, but not every session needs it. Most training happens at levels 1–4. Use it when:

    ✅ Athletes are proficient at lower levels 🧪 You want to stress-test skills 🏆 Preparing for competition

    Game-Specific Troubleshooting

    Athletes aren't switching roles in asymmetric games

    Problem: One side is dominating; roles aren't switching.

    1Check skill levelsMismatch may require an intensity adjustment.
    2Review constraintsAre the invariants being followed?
    3Adjust levelsDrop to an easier level.
    4Add constraintsGive the struggling side an edge (e.g. attacker must pause between strikes).

    Athletes are gaming the rules instead of developing skills

    Problem: Athletes find loopholes that win the game without developing the target skill.

    1Address mindsetReference the Training Mindset principle.
    2Close the loopholeAdd or clarify invariants.
    3Adjust the levelIt may be too easy or too hard.
    4Discuss with the athleteThey may not realize what they're doing.

    Athletes get frustrated when they can't 'win'

    Problem: A competitive mindset is interfering with learning.

    1Reframe successSuccess is learning, not winning.
    2Remove scorekeepingSome games work better without tracking.
    3Emphasize processCelebrate good attempts, not just results.
    4Reference principlesThe Training Mindset addresses this directly.

    Games feel chaotic or unstructured

    Problem: Athletes don't know what they're supposed to be doing.

    1Clarify objectivesEnsure everyone knows the goal.
    2DemonstrateShow what good play looks like.
    3Start simplerDrop to Level 1.
    4Check prerequisitesAthletes may lack foundational skills.

    Safety Questions

    How hard should contact be?

    Touch, contact made, no force→Skill isolation, beginners
    Light, force felt, not damaging→Most development games
    Moderate, significant force, controlled→Advanced games, integration

    When in doubt, go lighter. Quality over intensity.

    When should I stop a game?

    Stop immediately if:

    An athlete loses composure Contact becomes excessive Rules are being ignored An athlete appears hurt The learning environment breaks down

    Reset, address the issue, then resume.

    How do I manage aggression?

    Some athletes escalate intensity inappropriately:

    1Set expectationsClearly, before the game starts.
    2Match intensityPair athletes of similar intensity preference.
    3Call resetsInterrupt escalation early.
    4Address privatelyDiscuss mindset after the session.
    5Adjust pairingSome athletes shouldn't work together.

    Planning Questions

    How long should rounds be?

    Skill isolation→2–4 minutes
    Development games→4–6 minutes
    Combined games→3–5 minutes
    Integration→5–10 minutes

    Shorter rounds with more resets often beat long continuous play.

    How many games per session?

    Quality over quantity:

    🐣
    Beginners

    2–3 games, more time per game.

    🧗
    Intermediate

    3–4 games, balanced time.

    🥇
    Advanced

    Can handle more variety.

    One well-developed skill beats five superficial exposures.

    What if I don't have the right equipment?

    Adapt as needed, the constraints matter more than the gear:

    Wall / cage→Marked perimeter, padded wall, corner
    Mats→Grass, carpet, reduced ground work
    Gloves→Lighter contact, open-hand games
    Boundary markers→Tape, cones, imaginary lines

    Athlete Questions

    How do I know if I'm improving?

    Look for these markers:

    🔁 ConsistencyCan you repeat successful actions? 🎨 VarietyDo you have multiple solutions available? 🧘 ComposureAre you calmer under pressure? 👁️ RecognitionDo you see opportunities earlier? ↗️ TransferDoes the skill show up in sparring?

    What if I keep losing at a game?

    Losing is learning. Struggle is where growth happens.

    1AnalyzeWhat specifically is going wrong?
    2AskGet feedback from your partner or coach.
    3SimplifyDrop to an easier level.
    4FocusWork on one thing at a time.
    5PersistKeep going, that's where it forms.

    Reference: Training Mindset principle

    Should I always play to win?

    No. Games are academic exercises.

    • Learning matters more than winning
    • Honest play creates real feedback
    • Struggle is valuable
    • Your partner is your collaborator

    See: Training Mindset principle

    Coaching Scenarios

    How do I know when an athlete is ready for the next level?

    Look for these signs:

    ✅
    Consistent success

    They hit the game's objective most of the time.

    😴
    Boredom

    The current level no longer challenges them.

    ⚙️
    Automatic execution

    They don't think hard about the basics.

    🙋
    Asking for more

    They want additional challenge.

    When in doubt, stay at the current level longer. Mastery beats rushing.

    What if athletes in my group are at different levels?

    1Pair by levelSimilar-level athletes work together.
    2Handicap the advanced athleteAdd constraints (e.g. one hand only).
    3Role assignmentAdvanced athletes play feeder/attacker roles.
    4Split the groupRun different levels simultaneously.
    5Use it as teachingAdvanced athletes coach beginners between rounds.

    How do I introduce this system to a traditional gym?

    Gradual integration works best:

    1Start with one gamePick something fun like Touch Game.
    2Frame it as a warm-upLess threatening to existing culture.
    3Let results speakAthletes will notice skill transfer.
    4Add games slowlyOne new game every few weeks.
    5Don't criticize the old wayPresent games as "adding tools."

    Can I mix games with regular sparring?

    Yes, and you should. Games develop skills; sparring tests integration.

    Game → Sparring→Touch Game warm-up, then free sparring
    Themed sparring→Spar with one game's focus ("only score with counters")
    Game → Integration→Skill isolation, then open rounds

    What if an athlete has physical limitations?

    Adapt the game, not the athlete. The ecological approach embraces individual solutions.

    1Modify rangeCloser or farther based on mobility.
    2Reduce intensityLower contact levels.
    3Change objectivesFocus on what they CAN do.
    4Adjust timingMore time to execute.
    5Skip levelsNot every level suits every athlete.

    How do I handle a big skill gap between partners?

    🥇
    Advanced partner

    Provide honest resistance, work weak areas, focus on timing & reading, be a good training partner.

    Don't sandbag completely; use the off-hand or limit tools to stay challenged while creating learning opportunities.
    VS
    🐣
    Less experienced

    Focus on survival first, ask questions, celebrate small wins.

    Don't overreach, progress, not dominance, is the measure. Learn from the experience gap.

    What about athletes who are too passive?

    Passive athletes need encouragement to engage:

    1Simplify objectives"Just touch their shoulder once."
    2Remove consequencesPlay without scoring temporarily.
    3Pair with patient partnersAggressive opponents make it worse.
    4Celebrate attemptsReward action regardless of outcome.
    5Check for fearPassivity often masks uncertainty or anxiety.

    Do I need to follow prerequisite chains strictly?

    Guidelines, not rules. Use judgment, you know your athletes.

    • Prerequisites indicate logical progression, Skill A helps with Skill B
    • Experienced athletes can skip ahead, a wrestler doesn't need Takedown Defense basics
    • Return to prerequisites when stuck, regression often means foundation gaps

    See: Prerequisite Map

    Can I create my own game modifications?

    Absolutely.

    When modifying:

    1Keep the core problemDon't lose what the game teaches.
    2Maintain invariantsRules exist for reasons.
    3Test it firstTry it yourself before deploying.
    4Document changesTrack what works.
    5Share itOthers may benefit.

    How do I track athlete progress across games?

    Simple methods, but avoid over-measuring; learning isn't always linear or visible:

    🪜
    Level tracking

    Record highest level achieved per game.

    📊
    Confidence rating

    Athlete self-rates comfort (1–5).

    🎥
    Video comparison

    Record early vs. later performance.

    ↗️
    Sparring transfer

    Note when game skills appear in sparring.

    📝
    Coach notes

    Qualitative observations.
