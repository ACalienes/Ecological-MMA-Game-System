    Session Design Framework

    CLA MethodologyGROW ModelTwo DialsPeriodization

    ← Environment Design · CLA Index · MMA Applications →

    A session is a designed environment, not a list of drills. You set a goal, read where the athlete is, choose constraints to bridge the gap, then prepare to deliver it, and you scale difficulty with two independent dials, not one.

    Sources

    The GROW model and session planning frameworks are adapted from Renshaw et al. (2019). For complete bibliography, see Sources & Theoretical Foundations.

    The GROW Model

    G: Goal→What is the intention for this session? Establish primary focus.
    R: Reality→Where is the athlete now? Assess current skill level.
    O: Options→What activities will bridge the gap? Select practice environments.
    W: Way Forward→How will you implement? Plan delivery and preparation.

    GROW: The Detail

    🎯
    G: Goal

    The intention for the session and how it links to the overall goal.Ask: What is the goal/intention for this session? How does it link to the overall goal? What is the focus for learning?
    Consider: align coach & athlete intentions · connect to long-term development · identify the "rate limiter."

    📍
    R: Reality

    Where the athlete is now and what affordances to design in.Ask: What is the current skill level (Coordination or Adaptability)? What affordances need to be designed-in? Which? Why? When?
    Assess via observation, video, performance data, across physical, technical, tactical, psychological factors.

    🧩
    O: Options

    The activities and constraints that bridge the gap.Ask: What practice activities bridge the gap? Which practice environment? What constraints, and how will you manipulate them? How will you measure performance?

    🚀
    W: Way Forward

    How you prepare and deliver.Ask: How will you prepare the practice environment? How will you prepare the performers? Anything else needed to be ready?
    Prepare: physical setup · mental framing · cultural environment (values, beliefs, climate).

    Success Criteria (under Options)

    We will know we have been successful if…

    👁️
    We SEE

    Observable behaviors.

    📊
    The DATA SHOWS

    Measurable outcomes.

    🗣️
    The COACH SAYS

    Qualitative assessment.

    💭
    The PERFORMER FEELS

    Subjective experience.

    The Session Planner

    Session GoalsIntention · skill level (Coordination/Adaptability) · method (Explore/Exploit/Execute).
    Representativeness DialA 1–10 slider, Low → High.
    ConstraintsTask · Individual · Environment.
    Variability DialGreen Zone → Amber Zone → Red Zone (low → high instability).
    Task Descriptions1 · Pre-test / warm-up · 2 · Main activity · 3 · Progression / variation · 4 · Post-test / cool-down.

    Session Flow Pattern

    Green Zone→Confidence, build, assess baseline; athletes arrive in varied states, green calibrates them
    Amber Zone→Challenge, the productive instability needed for learning
    Green Zone→Consolidation, lock in the pattern, end the session positively
    Why this pattern: functional noise, and hysteresis

    The "productive instability" of the amber zone is what ecological psychology calls functional noise (Blau & Wagman, 2022, p. 242–243). Noise is not error: it constantly perturbs the system and is precisely what lets the "ball" escape a shallow, local attractor (a comfortable-but-suboptimal habit) and settle into a deeper, better one. In one study, added noise made participants more likely to discover the efficient strategy (p. 243). The amber zone is therefore deliberate, useful perturbation, not chaos to be avoided.

    The GREEN → AMBER → GREEN flow also respects hysteresis (p. 250): a system's current state depends on its history/trajectory, not just present conditions. A pattern entered under amber-zone pressure persists, so the order in which you pass through the zones matters, you cannot treat a session as path-independent. (See the Ecological Psychology section.)

    The 30-Second Game Plan

    A standing ritual before every game: after the rules are stated, athletes take 30 seconds to set a plan. The prompt: "What are the things I can genuinely test to solve this problem?" Not a script to execute, a hypothesis to run. When it fails, that's data: try something different on purpose, and cycle through options until one works.

    ⏱️
    Before the game

    30 seconds, one plan, stated to yourself.The plan makes the game a deliberate experiment instead of a brawl. It also exposes shark eyes early: if the plan is "land strikes" and the win condition is the wall, the plan is wrong.

    🔬
    During the game

    Treat failure as a result, not a verdict.The faster you realize the thing you did didn't work and try something different on purpose, the faster you cycle through options and find one that works. Frustration repeats; experiments iterate.

    🔍
    Test the stated rules

    Only the spoken constraints bind."Nobody said the elbows go on the ground. All I said was turtle." Athletes who probe the legal space of a rule set find solutions the coach didn't prescribe, exactly the point.

    Scaling Difficulty: The Two Dials

    📡
    Representativeness Dial (RLD)

    1 → 10. Low = modified, simplified. High = full competition.How closely practice samples the information of the competition environment. Turn it up to demand more competition-like reading and pressure.

    🌡️
    Variability Dial

    Green (stable/predictable) → Amber (productive instability) → Red (too much chaos).How much instability is in the environment. Amber is the target for learning; red overwhelms.

    Use them together: High RLD + Amber Zone = maximum learning transfer. Low RLD + Green Zone = safe exploration for beginners.

    The two dials are control parameters

    In dynamical-systems terms, the representativeness dial and the variability dial are control parameters, variables that, when turned, reorganize the fighter's attractor layout (Blau & Wagman, 2022, p. 250). Turning them does not dictate the new behavior; it changes which behaviors are stable. Other control parameters you turn in a session include speed, pressure, range, fatigue, and number of opponents. Expect that pushing one past a critical value produces an abrupt, nonlinear reorganization (a stance or rhythm suddenly switches) rather than a smooth change, design for that switch rather than being surprised by it. (See Theory and the Ecological Psychology section.)

    Constraint Manipulation During Sessions

    🔒
    When to tighten

    Channel and contain.Athletes lost or frustrated (too much chaos) · need to channel exploration in a specific direction · building confidence after failure · introducing a new concept.

    🔓
    When to loosen

    Open and test.Athletes succeeding too easily · patterns becoming rigid · need to test adaptability · building toward competition demands.

    Adjustment Signals

    High success, low effort→Too easy, increase challenge (loosen / add constraints)
    Frustration, repeated failure→Too hard, decrease challenge (tighten / remove constraints)
    Trying new solutions→Productive instability, maintain current level
    Rigid, repetitive patterns→System capture, increase variability

    The Four Session Types

    🌱
    Learning

    Developing new coordination patterns.Lower RLD (modified) · Green→Amber zones · higher acceptance of failure · exploration encouraged · coach more active in design.

    🔧
    Adapting

    Refining and stress-testing patterns.Moderate→High RLD · amber-zone emphasis · varied conditions · problem-solving required · coach observes and adjusts.

    🏆
    Performing

    Executing under representative pressure.High RLD (competition simulation) · Green→Amber zones · success orientation · full intensity · coach minimal intervention.

    🎚️
    Attuning

    Calibrating to a specific environment/opponent.Very high RLD (specific simulation) · green zone · familiarization · confidence building · used pre-competition.

    Session Debrief: Five Questions

    1. What were the goals and expectations? Intended affordances · success criteria.
    2. What actually happened? Design effectiveness · coaching delivery · learning observed.
    3. What went well and why? Learning-design successes · instruction/constraint effectiveness.
    4. What can be improved? Design modifications · constraint adjustments.
    5. What are the key takeaways? 3 things to continue · 3 things to change.

    Integration with Periodization

    1Off-SeasonLower RLD · higher variability · exploration, new skills.Attractor op: Learning, birth of new attractors. Expect exploration and temporary instability.
    2Pre-CampModerate RLD · moderate variability · building specific patterns.Attractor op: Learning → adaptation transition.
    3Fight CampHigher RLD · lower variability · refining for the opponent.Attractor op: Adaptation, moving an existing attractor to this opponent's context.
    4Fight WeekHighest RLD · lowest variability · execution, confidence.Attractor op: Adaptation, stabilize the moved attractor.
    Learning vs. adaptation across the cycle

    Periodization maps cleanly onto the dynamical distinction (Blau & Wagman, 2022, p. 241). Off-season is learning, the birth of a new attractor, so expect exploration and temporary instability. Fight camp is adaptation, the movement of an existing attractor to fit a specific opponent, not the acquisition of something new. Reserve the words accordingly: do not call fight-camp refinement "learning, " and do not expect off-season instability during fight week. (See Theory.)
