    Environment Design Principles (EDP)

    CLA Knowledge BaseThe Core MethodologyFour Principles

    Navigation: ← Key Concepts | CLA Index | Session Design →

    Sources

    The four Environment Design Principles are the core methodology from Renshaw et al. (2019). Representative Learning Design draws on Pinder et al. (2011). Repetition Without Repetition originates with Bernstein (1967). For the complete bibliography, see Sources & Theoretical Foundations.

    The EDP framework operationalizes CLA into four actionable principles that guide every coaching decision in the ecological approach.

    1️⃣
    Session Intention

    The overriding, organizational constraint.

    2️⃣
    Constrain to Afford

    Invite the behaviors you want.

    3️⃣
    Representative Learning Design

    Practice looks AND feels like competition.

    4️⃣
    Repetition Without Repetition

    Repeat the problem, not the movement.

    Principle 1 · Session Intention

    "The intentions of the session act as an overriding and organizational constraint." , Renshaw et al., 2019

    • Intentions shape cognitions, perceptions, and actions.
    • Coach and athlete intentions must be aligned.
    • Intentions determine constraint-manipulation decisions.
    • Intentions vary with training phase (pre-season vs. competition week).

    Session intention categories

    🌱
    Learning

    Develop new skills/patterns.Higher challenge, acceptance of failure.

    🎚️
    Adapting

    Refine existing patterns.Varied conditions, problem-solving.

    🔥
    Performing

    Execute under pressure.Representative conditions, full intensity.

    🎯
    Attuning

    Calibrate to the environment.Familiarization, preparation.

    Matching intentions to learning stage

    Coordination Stage→Exploring, searching for solutions
    Adaptability Stage→Exploiting, refining, optimizing

    For MMA: A fighter learning takedown defense (coordination stage) needs sessions exploring defensive options. A fighter refining it (adaptability stage) needs varied attackers and conditions to stress-test their solutions.

    Principle 2 · Constrain to Afford

    "Design-in constraints to offer / invite / encourage learners to explore opportunities for action related to the session intention." , Renshaw et al., 2019

    Offer→Key affordances related to session intention
    Invite→Engagement through exaggeration/emphasis
    Avoid→Over-constraining (forcing vs. inviting)

    The outlet analogy: a good constraint affords, it does not prescribe

    Ecological psychology gives the cleanest test for a good constraint (Blau & Wagman, 2022, p. 271). Compare two ways to wire a building:

    📍
    "Put an outlet exactly here"

    Other-organization. One acceptable outcome, dictated from outside.

    📐
    "Outlets every 12 feet"

    A local rule. Infinite valid layouts, "the solution will emerge."

    A good constraint is always the second kind. "Defend the takedown, points for staying standing" is "outlets every 12 feet"; "when they shoot you must sprawl" is "outlet exactly here." If a constraint forces one specific solution, it is over-constraining. See the Ecological Psychology section.

    Constraining strategies

    🔍
    Exaggeration

    Amplify affordances to make them obvious.Larger targets, slower speeds.

    📣
    Emphasis

    Draw attention to specific affordances.Reward systems, verbal cues.

    🚧
    Restriction

    Limit options to channel exploration.Reduce techniques available.

    ➕
    Addition

    Add elements to create affordances.Include opponents, obstacles.

    Over-constraining vs. effective constraining

    "Two-touch only" rules→Reward systems for quick passing
    Forced technique prescription→Environmental setup that invites technique
    Removing all decision-making→Channeling decisions within options
    Static, unopposed drilling→Dynamic, contextual practice

    Co-adaptation principle: Constrain the opposition to facilitate behavior change in your athletes. To develop counter-striking, constrain the attacker to initiate first, the defender (primary learner) must then read and respond. The constraint on one creates the learning opportunity for the other.

    Principle 3 · Representative Learning Design (RLD)

    "Ensure that what learners are seeing, hearing, and feeling in the practice environment is similar to the performance environment." , Renshaw et al., 2019; RLD framework by Pinder, Davids, Renshaw & Araújo (2011)

    🎯
    Action fidelity

    The core concept."The movement solutions performers develop in practice must be effective when transferred to the performance environment."

    ❓
    The core question

    Does practice sample what matters?"Does the practice environment sample the critical informational constraints present in the performance environment?"

    RLD assessment questions

    • Does practice contain relevant informational constraints from performance?
    • Is perception-action coupling maintained (not decomposed)?
    • Are the emotional demands represented (Affective Learning Design)?
    • Will learned behaviors transfer to competition?

    The RLD continuum

    Low representativeness
    modified conditions, simplified context    ↔High representativeness
    full competition simulation

    Neither extreme is always appropriate, match RLD to athlete needs.

    RLD assessment checklist

    • Information sources, are relevant visual, haptic, acoustic cues present?
    • Perception-action coupling, is perceiving and acting integrated (not separated)?
    • Decision-making, are the decisions required similar to competition?
    • Opponents/partners, are human interactions included (not static objects)?
    • Emotional intensity, is appropriate pressure/consequence present?
    • Time constraints, is temporal pressure representative?
    • Space constraints, is the spatial setup representative?

    Task simplification vs. task decomposition

    ✅
    Simplification: preferred

    Scale difficulty while keeping the task whole.Maintains perception-action coupling, preserves essential information flows. Example: smaller cage, slower pace.

    ⚠️
    Decomposition: problematic

    Break the task into isolated parts.Decouples perception from action, removes context and information. Example: shadow boxing without a target.

    Key distinction: Simplification keeps the task intact but adjusts difficulty. Decomposition breaks the task apart, destroying the perception-action relationship.

    What reduces RLD (avoid these)

    Dribbling around conesNo adaptive opponent information. Hitting pads without partner reactionDecoupled perception-action. Unopposed drillingNo defensive information to read. Ball machines / predictable feedsMissing anticipatory cues. Static positional workNo dynamic decision-making.

    What enhances RLD (implement these)

    Live training partners→Real affordances, adaptation
    Varied opposition styles→Multiple solutions required
    Consequence systems→Emotional representativeness
    Competition simulation→Full pressure experience
    Contextual drilling→Maintains perception-action coupling

    Affective Learning Design (ALD): "Does the practice look AND feel like the real thing?" Built on Purpose (clear, meaningful goals), Consequence (real outcomes for success/failure), and Emotion (representative intensity and pressure). Sparring looks like fighting, but adding scoring, consequences, or competition formats makes it feel like it.

    Principle 4 · Repetition Without Repetition

    "Practice is a particular type of repetition without repetition." , Bernstein, 1967. Repeat the process of solving problems, not identical movements. Design-in the appropriate amount of variability and (in)stability.

    Variability is the feature, not the bug: the infant-walking robot tournament

    The strongest evidence that variable practice beats regular practice comes from a simulation tournament (Blau & Wagman, 2022, p. 169–170). Robots trained on highly variable infant-walking paths were pitted against robots trained on regular, repetitive paths in a 4,000-game tournament. The variable-trained robots won 2,888 to 75. Variability does not degrade the skill, it builds the adaptable, degenerate system that copes with novelty. See the Ecological Psychology section.

    Types of practice variability

    🎯
    Task variability

    Varying the task performed.Different techniques for the same goal.

    🌍
    Environmental variability

    Varying conditions.Different opponents, surfaces.

    🧍
    Individual variability

    Varying performer state.Fatigued, fresh, stressed.

    Sources of variability: how to implement

    Task goals, vary objectives→Submit vs. survive vs. control
    Rules, modify regulations→No takedowns, no striking
    Opponents, different partners→Size, style, skill level
    Environment, change conditions→Cage vs. mats, fresh vs. fatigued
    Equipment, alter gear→Different glove weights
    Timing, adjust duration→Round length, rest periods
    Intensity, vary effort→Light flow to hard sparring

    Variability zones

    🟢
    Green · Optimal challenge

    High success likelihood.Promotes exploration, enhances adaptability.

    🟡
    Amber · Productive instability

    Requires search for new solutions.Promotes reorganization, the edge of chaos, optimal for learning.

    🔴
    Red · Excessive chaos

    Too much variability.Unmanageable / overwhelming, information overload.

    Matching variability to skill level

    Beginner / Coordination→Low variability, systematic (controlled), guided toward solutions
    Intermediate→Moderate variability, mixed
    Advanced / Adaptability→High variability, unsystematic (random), challenge to explore

    Systematic manipulation (controlled, progressive constraint changes) targets specific adaptations. Unsystematic manipulation (random, varied changes) develops general adaptability.

    Why variability matters

    🤹
    Develops dexterity

    Multiple solutions to problems.

    🔁
    Builds degeneracy

    Functional redundancy.

    🔄
    Enhances adaptability

    Coping with novel situations.

    🔓
    Prevents system capture

    Avoiding locked-in patterns.

    Managing instability

    Deliberately move performers to less stable areas to promote reorganization. Instability is necessary for learning, not a failure state.

    📊
    Instability indicators

    How to recognize productive instability.Increased performance variability, temporary performance decrement, search behaviors (trying new solutions), frustration (productive when managed).

    🧑‍🏫
    Coach response

    How to support the transition.Recognize instability as necessary for learning, support through the transition, allow time for reorganization, celebrate exploration, not just success.
