    Glossary of Terms

    CLA MethodologyA–V ReferenceHover for Definition

    ← Pitfalls · CLA Index · Quick Reference →

    The shared vocabulary of the system. Every term traces to ecological dynamics, affordances, synergies, constraints, attractors. Hover or tap any term for its full definition.

    Sources

    Definitions adapted from Renshaw et al. (2019), Gibson (1979), Bernstein (1967), Newell (1986), and Kelso (1995). For complete bibliography, see Sources & Theoretical Foundations.

    A

    📐 Action FidelityThe degree to which movement solutions developed in practice will be effective in the performance environment. 🔄 AdaptationThe process by which behavior becomes better suited to a specific environment. 💓 Affective Learning Design (ALD)Practice design that represents the emotional demands of competition, ensuring practice not only looks like but feels like the real thing. 🎁 AffordanceAn opportunity for action that the environment offers relative to an individual's capabilities. Affordances are relational (between person and environment), action-relevant, and can be perceived directly. 🗺️ Affordance LandscapeThe range of opportunities for action available in a practice or performance environment, ranging from narrow (few options) to rich (many options). 🟠 Amber ZoneA variability zone representing productive instability at the "edge of chaos" where new solutions must be searched for, reorganization is promoted, and optimal learning occurs. 🎯 AttunementThe process of becoming increasingly sensitive to relevant information in the environment.

    C

    🎛️ CalibrationAdjusting the relationship between perceived information and action execution to achieve desired outcomes. 🤝 Co-AdaptationThe continuous mutual adjustment between interacting individuals, such as attacker and defender in combat sports. 🕸️ Complex Adaptive SystemA system composed of many interacting components that can adapt and self-organize. Athletes and teams are viewed as complex adaptive systems in CLA. 🚧 ConstraintsInformation sources that shape the emergence of behavior; boundaries that guide organization. Constraints are not negative limitations but neutral features that reduce possible configurations to functional ones. 🧭 Constraints-Led Approach (CLA)A coaching methodology based on manipulating task, individual, and environmental constraints to guide skill development through self-organization rather than prescription. 🔗 Coordinative StructureSee Synergy.

    D

    ♻️ DegeneracyThe ability of structurally different elements to achieve the same functional outcome. In MMA, having multiple techniques that can accomplish the same goal (e.g., various ways to achieve a takedown). 🔢 Degrees of FreedomThe independent components (muscles, joints, limb segments) that must be organized for coordinated action. The "degrees of freedom problem" refers to the challenge of organizing these many components. 🤹 DexterityThe ability to find motor solutions that are correct, quick, rational, and resourceful. Reflects the ability to solve novel movement problems in the moment. 👥 Dyadic SystemA two-person interaction unit (e.g., attacker-defender pair) that forms the basic unit of analysis in combat sports.

    E

    🌐 Ecological DynamicsThe theoretical framework integrating ecological psychology, dynamical systems theory, and complexity science that underlies CLA. 💪 EffectivityAn individual's capability to act on environmental affordances. The complement to affordance (environment offers, individual can use). ✨ EmergenceNovel behaviors arising from the interaction of system components, not prescribed or pre-planned. In CLA, technique emerges from constraints rather than being taught directly. 📐 Environment Design Principles (EDP)Framework of four principles for CLA session design: Session Intention, Constrain to Afford, Representative Learning Design, and Repetition Without Repetition. 🌍 Environmental ConstraintsGlobal features external to the task that shape behavior, including physical conditions (temperature, surface), social factors (culture, crowd), and historical factors (traditions, rule evolution).

    F

    📍 Fixed PrinciplesSignature positions or anchors that functional movements tend to pass through, representing stable states in coordination. 🏛️ Form of LifeThe behaviors, skills, attitudes, and practices that shape culture in a society, organization, or training environment. 🧊 Freezing Degrees of FreedomA strategy in early learning where the performer locks out movement options to reduce complexity, resulting in stiff or rigid movement patterns.

    G

    🟢 Green ZoneA variability zone representing optimal challenge where success likelihood is high, exploration is promoted, and adaptability can develop without overwhelming the learner. 📋 GROW ModelGoal-Reality-Options-Way Forward planning framework adapted for CLA session design.

    I

    👤 Individual ConstraintsPersonal characteristics unique to each performer that shape behavior, including physical (height, weight), psychological (motivation, confidence), experiential (skill level, history), and biological (fatigue, injury) factors. Also called Organismic Constraints. 🔗 Individual-Environment MutualityThe inseparable relationship between performer and performance context. The fundamental premise of ecological dynamics. 〰️ InstabilityTemporary perturbation of existing patterns, often necessary for reorganization and learning. Distinguished from stability (consistent patterns) and chaos (excessive disruption). 🎯 IntentionA goal-directed cognitive state that shapes perception-action coupling. Session intention is the overriding goal that guides constraint manipulation. 🧬 Intrinsic DynamicsExisting coordination tendencies an individual brings to a learning situation, which interact with task demands to produce behavior.

    K

    📖 Knowledge ABOUTDeclarative, verbalizable understanding of the environment, gained through instruction, video, and discussion. 🤸 Knowledge OFProcedural, action-supporting understanding developed only through direct experience in the environment. Cannot be taught verbally.

    N

    🌀 Nonlinear PedagogyThe pedagogical framework derived from ecological dynamics that serves as the bridge between theory and CLA methodology. 📈 NonlinearityDisproportionate relationship between inputs and outputs; small changes can have large effects, and large changes can have small effects. Learning is nonlinear.

    O

    🤲 Optimal GripHaving functional control over the field of affordances in a given context. The hallmark of skilled performance. 👤 Organismic ConstraintsSee Individual Constraints. 🔒 Over-ConstrainingManipulating constraints to force rather than invite behavior, removing decision-making and exploration.

    P

    🔁 Perception-Action CouplingThe tight integration of information pickup and movement execution. Information regulates action; action guides further information pickup. A continuous loop, not sequential processes.

    R

    ⏳ Rate LimiterA constraint that currently limits the emergence of higher-level performance. Identifying the rate limiter helps target training. 🔴 Red ZoneA variability zone representing excessive chaos where variability is unmanageable, overwhelm occurs, and learning is disrupted. 🔄 Repetition Without RepetitionBernstein's principle that practice should repeat the process of problem-solving rather than identical movements. Variation in execution while repeating the goal. 📡 Representative Learning Design (RLD)Practice design that samples critical informational features of the performance environment, ensuring what athletes see, hear, and feel in practice is similar to competition.

    S

    🌱 Self-OrganizationSpontaneous emergence of coordinated patterns without external micromanagement. Behavior organizes itself under constraints rather than being prescribed. 🎯 Session IntentionThe overriding goal that guides constraint manipulation in a training session. One of the four EDP principles. 🤝 Shared AffordancesOpportunities for action that exist between multiple individuals, as in partner or team training. ⚡ SkillIn ecological dynamics, the ability to successfully adapt to a performance environment. Defined as relationship between performer and environment, not as internal acquisition. 🧩 Soft AssemblyThe temporary, context-dependent formation of coordinative structures. Synergies are soft assembled (not hardwired) and can rapidly form and dissolve. 🎼 SynergyA temporarily assembled grouping of body segments, muscles, and joints that function as a single unit to achieve a task goal. Also called Coordinative Structure.

    T

    📋 Task ConstraintsFeatures specific to the activity being performed that shape behavior, including goals, rules, equipment, spatial boundaries, and time limits. ✂️ Task DecompositionBreaking a task into isolated parts for separate practice. Problematic in CLA because it separates perception from action. 🎚️ Task SimplificationScaling difficulty while keeping the task whole. Preferred over decomposition because it maintains perception-action coupling. ➡️ TransferThe application of learning from one context (training) to another (competition). The ultimate measure of training effectiveness.

    V

    🎲 VariabilityDifferences in movement patterns or practice conditions. In CLA, variability is deliberately designed into practice to build adaptability. 🚦 Variability ZonesCategories (Green/Amber/Red) describing the challenge level of practice environments, from stable and predictable to chaotic and overwhelming.
